# Correspondence Theorem

The correspondence theorem (also called the **lattice isomorphism theorem** or **fourth isomorphism theorem**) gives a complete description of the subgroup structure of a quotient group $G/N$ in terms of the subgroups of $G$ that contain $N$.

---

## Statement

<Theorem title="Correspondence Theorem (Lattice Isomorphism Theorem)" number="2.23">
Let $N \trianglelefteq G$ and $\pi : G \to G/N$ be the canonical projection. There is an inclusion-preserving bijection

$$\Phi : \{H \leq G \mid N \leq H\} \xrightarrow{\;1:1\;} \{K \leq G/N\}$$

given by $\Phi(H) = H/N = \pi(H)$, with inverse $\Psi(K) = \pi^{-1}(K)$.

Moreover, this bijection preserves:
1. **Inclusion**: $H_1 \leq H_2$ iff $H_1/N \leq H_2/N$.
2. **Normality**: $H \trianglelefteq G$ iff $H/N \trianglelefteq G/N$ (for $N \leq H$).
3. **Index**: $[G : H] = [G/N : H/N]$.
4. **Intersections and joins**: $\Phi(H_1 \cap H_2) = \Phi(H_1) \cap \Phi(H_2)$ and $\Phi(\langle H_1, H_2 \rangle) = \langle \Phi(H_1), \Phi(H_2) \rangle$.
</Theorem>

---

## Proof

<Proof>
**Well-defined**: If $N \leq H \leq G$, then $N \trianglelefteq H$ (since $N \trianglelefteq G$ and $H \leq G$), so $H/N$ is a subgroup of $G/N$.

**Inverse map**: For $K \leq G/N$, define $\Psi(K) = \pi^{-1}(K) = \{g \in G \mid gN \in K\}$. Since $\pi$ is a homomorphism, $\Psi(K) \leq G$. Also $N = \pi^{-1}(\{eN\}) \leq \pi^{-1}(K) = \Psi(K)$.

**$\Phi$ and $\Psi$ are mutually inverse**: For $H$ with $N \leq H$: $\Psi(\Phi(H)) = \pi^{-1}(H/N) = H$ (since $g \in \pi^{-1}(H/N)$ iff $gN \in H/N$ iff $g \in H$). For $K \leq G/N$: $\Phi(\Psi(K)) = \pi(\pi^{-1}(K)) = K$ (since $\pi$ is surjective).

**Preserves normality**: $H \trianglelefteq G$ iff $gHg^{-1} = H$ for all $g \in G$ iff $(gN)(H/N)(gN)^{-1} = H/N$ for all $gN \in G/N$ iff $H/N \trianglelefteq G/N$.

**Preserves index**: $[G : H] = |G|/|H| = (|G|/|N|)/(|H|/|N|) = |G/N|/|H/N| = [G/N : H/N]$ (for finite groups; the general case uses the bijection between coset spaces).
</Proof>

## Examples

<Example id="z12-correspondence" title="Subgroups of Z/12Z via Z">
Take $G = \mathbb{Z}$, $N = 12\mathbb{Z}$. The subgroups of $G$ containing $N$ are $d\mathbb{Z}$ where $d \mid 12$: namely $\mathbb{Z}, 2\mathbb{Z}, 3\mathbb{Z}, 4\mathbb{Z}, 6\mathbb{Z}, 12\mathbb{Z}$.

By correspondence, the subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$ are:

| Subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}$ containing $12\mathbb{Z}$ | Corresponding subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$ | Order |
|---|---|---|
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$ | 12 |
| $2\mathbb{Z}$ | $2\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} = \langle \bar{2} \rangle$ | 6 |
| $3\mathbb{Z}$ | $3\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} = \langle \bar{3} \rangle$ | 4 |
| $4\mathbb{Z}$ | $4\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} = \langle \bar{4} \rangle$ | 3 |
| $6\mathbb{Z}$ | $6\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} = \langle \bar{6} \rangle$ | 2 |
| $12\mathbb{Z}$ | $\{\bar{0}\}$ | 1 |

This recovers the well-known fact: subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ biject with divisors of $n$.
</Example>

<Example id="z36-mod6" title="Subgroups of Z/36Z containing 6Z/36Z">
The subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}/36\mathbb{Z}$ that contain $\langle \bar{6} \rangle = 6\mathbb{Z}/36\mathbb{Z}$ correspond to subgroups $d\mathbb{Z}$ of $\mathbb{Z}$ with $6\mathbb{Z} \leq d\mathbb{Z} \leq \mathbb{Z}$, i.e., $d \mid 6$. So $d \in \{1, 2, 3, 6\}$, giving four subgroups:

$$\mathbb{Z}/36\mathbb{Z}, \quad \langle \bar{2} \rangle, \quad \langle \bar{3} \rangle, \quad \langle \bar{6} \rangle.$$

The quotient $(\mathbb{Z}/36\mathbb{Z})/\langle \bar{6} \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$, and the subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ are $\langle \bar{1} \rangle, \langle \bar{2} \rangle, \langle \bar{3} \rangle, \langle \bar{0} \rangle$ — exactly four, matching.
</Example>

<Example id="s3-a3-correspondence" title="Correspondence for S₃/A₃">
$A_3 \trianglelefteq S_3$. The subgroups of $S_3$ containing $A_3$ are: $A_3$ and $S_3$ (since $A_3$ has index 2, there is no subgroup strictly between them).

The quotient $S_3/A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ has subgroups $\{\bar{0}\}$ and $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ — exactly two, matching the correspondence.
</Example>

<Example id="s4-v4-correspondence" title="Correspondence for S₄/V₄">
$V_4 \trianglelefteq S_4$, $S_4/V_4 \cong S_3$. The subgroups of $S_3$ are $\{e\}$, three copies of $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, one $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$, and $S_3$ — six in total.

By correspondence, there are exactly six subgroups of $S_4$ containing $V_4$. These are:
- $V_4$ (corresponding to $\{e\}$ in $S_3$)
- $D_4^{(1)}, D_4^{(2)}, D_4^{(3)}$ — three copies of $D_4$ of order 8 (corresponding to the three $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$'s)
- $A_4$ of order 12 (corresponding to $A_3$)
- $S_4$ (corresponding to $S_3$)
</Example>

<Example id="normal-correspondence" title="Normal subgroups correspond">
The normal subgroups of $G/N$ are exactly $M/N$ where $M \trianglelefteq G$ and $N \leq M$. For example:

In $S_4$, the normal subgroups are $\{e\}, V_4, A_4, S_4$. The normal subgroups of $S_4$ containing $V_4$ are $V_4, A_4, S_4$. So the normal subgroups of $S_4/V_4 \cong S_3$ are $\{e\}, A_3, S_3$ — which is correct.
</Example>

## Application: counting subgroups

<Example id="counting-z-pq" title="Subgroups of Z/pqZ">
For distinct primes $p, q$: the subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}/pq\mathbb{Z}$ are $\langle \bar{d} \rangle$ for $d \mid pq$, i.e., $d \in \{1, p, q, pq\}$. There are exactly 4 subgroups, forming the lattice:

$$\mathbb{Z}/pq\mathbb{Z}$$
$$\;/\;\qquad\;\backslash$$
$$\langle \bar{p} \rangle \qquad\quad \langle \bar{q} \rangle$$
$$\;\backslash\;\qquad\;/$$
$$\{\bar{0}\}$$
</Example>

<Example id="index-preserving" title="Index is preserved">
If $N \leq H \leq G$ with $N \trianglelefteq G$, then $[G : H] = [G/N : H/N]$.

For $G = \mathbb{Z}$, $N = 12\mathbb{Z}$, $H = 4\mathbb{Z}$: $[\mathbb{Z} : 4\mathbb{Z}] = 4$ and $[\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} : 4\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}] = 12/3 = 4$. Check.
</Example>

## Connection to the third isomorphism theorem

<Remark title="Third isomorphism theorem as consequence">
The correspondence theorem implies the third isomorphism theorem. If $N \leq M$ with both normal in $G$, then $M/N \trianglelefteq G/N$ by the correspondence, and the quotient $(G/N)/(M/N)$ has order $[G/N : M/N] = [G : M] = |G/M|$. The natural map $(G/N)/(M/N) \to G/M$ given by $(gN)(M/N) \mapsto gM$ is the desired isomorphism.
</Remark>

<Example id="chain-quotients" title="A chain of quotients">
Consider $\mathbb{Z} \supset 2\mathbb{Z} \supset 6\mathbb{Z} \supset 12\mathbb{Z}$. Quotienting by $12\mathbb{Z}$:

$$\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} \supset 2\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} \supset 6\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} \supset \{\bar{0}\}.$$

By the third isomorphism theorem:
- $(\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z})/(2\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$
- $(2\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z})/(6\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}) \cong 2\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$
- $6\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$

So $|\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}| = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 = 12$. The quotient "factors" through the chain of normal subgroups.
</Example>

<Example id="q8-correspondence" title="Correspondence for Q₈/Z(Q₈)">
$Q_8 = \{\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\}$, $Z(Q_8) = \{1, -1\}$, $Q_8/Z(Q_8) \cong V_4$ (Klein four-group). The subgroups of $V_4$ are $\{e\}$, three copies of $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, and $V_4$ itself — five total.

By correspondence, the subgroups of $Q_8$ containing $Z(Q_8)$ are: $Z(Q_8)$, $\langle i \rangle = \{1, -1, i, -i\}$, $\langle j \rangle = \{1, -1, j, -j\}$, $\langle k \rangle = \{1, -1, k, -k\}$, and $Q_8$ — exactly five.
</Example>

<Example id="dn-correspondence" title="Correspondence for D_n/⟨r⟩ when n is odd">
For odd $n$, the rotation subgroup $\langle r \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is the unique subgroup of index 2, hence normal. The quotient $D_n/\langle r \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ has only two subgroups: $\{e\}$ and $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

By correspondence, the subgroups of $D_n$ containing $\langle r \rangle$ are: $\langle r \rangle$ and $D_n$ — exactly two. This confirms that there is no subgroup strictly between $\langle r \rangle$ and $D_n$ (which is clear since the index is prime).
</Example>

<Example id="maximal-correspondence" title="Maximal subgroups via correspondence">
A subgroup $M$ is **maximal** in $G$ if $M < G$ and there is no subgroup strictly between $M$ and $G$. By the correspondence theorem: maximal subgroups of $G$ containing $N$ correspond bijectively to maximal subgroups of $G/N$.

For $G = \mathbb{Z}$ and $N = 30\mathbb{Z}$: the maximal subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}/30\mathbb{Z}$ are $\langle \bar{2} \rangle, \langle \bar{3} \rangle, \langle \bar{5} \rangle$ (of index 2, 3, 5 respectively). These correspond to $2\mathbb{Z}, 3\mathbb{Z}, 5\mathbb{Z}$ in $\mathbb{Z}$ — the maximal subgroups containing $30\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

<Example id="simple-correspondence" title="Simple quotients">
$G/N$ is **simple** (has no nontrivial proper normal subgroups) if and only if there is no normal subgroup $M$ of $G$ with $N \subsetneq M \subsetneq G$. By the correspondence theorem, this means $N$ is a **maximal normal subgroup** of $G$.

For $G = S_4$, $N = V_4$: $S_4/V_4 \cong S_3$, which is not simple ($A_3 \trianglelefteq S_3$). Indeed, $A_4$ is a normal subgroup of $S_4$ with $V_4 \subsetneq A_4 \subsetneq S_4$.

For $G = S_4$, $N = A_4$: $S_4/A_4 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, which is simple. Indeed, $A_4$ is a maximal normal subgroup of $S_4$.
</Example>

## Summary

<Remark title="Key takeaways">
1. The correspondence theorem gives a **bijection** between subgroups of $G/N$ and subgroups of $G$ containing $N$.
2. The bijection preserves inclusion, normality, index, meets, and joins — it is a **lattice isomorphism**.
3. This is the essential tool for understanding the structure of quotient groups in terms of the original group.
4. Combined with the isomorphism theorems, it provides a complete picture of how subgroups, normal subgroups, and quotients interact.
</Remark>
