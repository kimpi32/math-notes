# The Isomorphism Theorems

The three isomorphism theorems (also called the "homomorphism theorems") are the fundamental structural results of group theory. They describe how quotient groups, subgroups, and homomorphisms interact.

---

## First Isomorphism Theorem

<Theorem title="First Isomorphism Theorem" number="2.20">
Let $\varphi : G \to H$ be a group homomorphism. Then:

1. $\ker(\varphi) \trianglelefteq G$.
2. $\operatorname{im}(\varphi) \leq H$.
3. The induced map $\bar{\varphi} : G/\ker(\varphi) \to \operatorname{im}(\varphi)$ defined by $\bar{\varphi}(g\ker(\varphi)) = \varphi(g)$ is an isomorphism.

In short: $G/\ker(\varphi) \cong \operatorname{im}(\varphi)$.
</Theorem>

<Remark title="The diamond diagram">
The first isomorphism theorem is often visualized as a commutative diagram:

$$G \xrightarrow{\;\varphi\;} H$$
$$\downarrow\pi \qquad \uparrow\iota$$
$$G/\ker(\varphi) \xrightarrow[\;\bar{\varphi}\;]{\sim} \operatorname{im}(\varphi)$$

where $\pi$ is the projection, $\iota$ is the inclusion, and $\bar{\varphi}$ is the isomorphism.
</Remark>

For the full proof, see the [dedicated proof page](/modern-algebra/ch02-subgroups-homomorphisms/proofs/p01-first-isomorphism-proof).

<Example id="z-to-zn" title="Z → Z/nZ">
The map $\varphi : \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$, $k \mapsto \bar{k}$, is surjective with $\ker(\varphi) = n\mathbb{Z}$. The first isomorphism theorem gives

$$\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}.$$

This is a tautology here, but it confirms consistency: $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ as defined by the quotient construction equals $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ as defined by modular arithmetic.
</Example>

<Example id="det-first-iso" title="det: GL → F*">
$\det : \mathrm{GL}(n, F) \to F^*$ is surjective with $\ker(\det) = \mathrm{SL}(n, F)$. By the first isomorphism theorem:

$$\mathrm{GL}(n, F)/\mathrm{SL}(n, F) \cong F^*.$$
</Example>

<Example id="sign-first-iso" title="sgn: Sₙ → {±1}">
$\mathrm{sgn} : S_n \to \{+1, -1\}$ is surjective (for $n \geq 2$) with $\ker = A_n$. So:

$$S_n/A_n \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}.$$
</Example>

<Example id="exp-first-iso" title="exp: R → R>0">
$\exp : (\mathbb{R}, +) \to (\mathbb{R}^{>0}, \times)$ is an isomorphism ($\ker = \{0\}$, surjective). The first isomorphism theorem gives $\mathbb{R}/\{0\} \cong \mathbb{R}^{>0}$, which is just $\mathbb{R} \cong \mathbb{R}^{>0}$.
</Example>

<Example id="complex-exp" title="Complex exponential">
$\exp : (\mathbb{R}, +) \to (S^1, \cdot)$, $\theta \mapsto e^{2\pi i \theta}$, is surjective with $\ker = \mathbb{Z}$. So:

$$\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z} \cong S^1.$$

This is the fundamental group-theoretic reason why the circle is $\mathbb{R} \bmod \mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

<Example id="abelianization" title="Abelianization">
The projection $\pi : G \to G/G'$ (where $G' = [G,G]$ is the commutator subgroup) gives $G/G'$ as the **abelianization** of $G$. Any homomorphism $\varphi : G \to A$ to an abelian group factors through $G/G'$.

For $G = S_3$: $G' = A_3$, so $S_3^{\mathrm{ab}} = S_3/A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

## Second Isomorphism Theorem

<Theorem title="Second Isomorphism Theorem (Diamond Theorem)" number="2.21">
Let $H \leq G$ and $N \trianglelefteq G$. Then:

1. $HN = \{hn \mid h \in H, n \in N\}$ is a subgroup of $G$.
2. $H \cap N \trianglelefteq H$.
3. $H/(H \cap N) \cong HN/N$.

The isomorphism is given by $h(H \cap N) \mapsto hN$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
Consider the composition $H \hookrightarrow G \xrightarrow{\pi} G/N$, i.e., $\varphi : H \to G/N$ defined by $\varphi(h) = hN$.

$\varphi$ is a homomorphism. Its kernel is $\ker(\varphi) = \{h \in H \mid hN = N\} = \{h \in H \mid h \in N\} = H \cap N$.

Its image is $\{hN \mid h \in H\} = HN/N$ (since every coset $hnN = hN$).

By the first isomorphism theorem: $H/(H \cap N) = H/\ker(\varphi) \cong \operatorname{im}(\varphi) = HN/N$.
</Proof>

<Remark title="Why 'Diamond'?">
The subgroup lattice diagram looks like a diamond:

$$HN$$
$$\;/\;\quad\;\backslash$$
$$H \qquad\quad N$$
$$\;\backslash\;\quad\;/$$
$$H \cap N$$

The second isomorphism theorem says the "left side" $H/(H \cap N)$ is isomorphic to the "right side" $HN/N$.
</Remark>

<Example id="second-iso-z" title="Second isomorphism theorem in Z">
$G = \mathbb{Z}$, $H = m\mathbb{Z}$, $N = n\mathbb{Z}$. Then $HN = m\mathbb{Z} + n\mathbb{Z} = \gcd(m,n)\mathbb{Z}$ and $H \cap N = \mathrm{lcm}(m,n)\mathbb{Z}$. The theorem gives:

$$m\mathbb{Z}/\mathrm{lcm}(m,n)\mathbb{Z} \cong \gcd(m,n)\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}.$$

For $m = 4$, $n = 6$: $4\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} \cong 2\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$, both isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

<Example id="second-iso-s4" title="Second isomorphism theorem in S₄">
$G = S_4$, $N = V_4 = \{e, (12)(34), (13)(24), (14)(23)\} \trianglelefteq S_4$, $H = S_3$ (permutations of $\{1,2,3\}$). Then $H \cap N = \{e\}$ (no nontrivial element of $V_4$ fixes 4 and lies in $S_3$). So:

$$S_3/\{e\} \cong S_3 V_4/V_4,$$

giving $|S_3 V_4| = |S_3| \cdot |V_4| / |S_3 \cap V_4| = 6 \cdot 4/1 = 24 = |S_4|$, so $S_3 V_4 = S_4$ and $S_4/V_4 \cong S_3$.
</Example>

## Third Isomorphism Theorem

<Theorem title="Third Isomorphism Theorem" number="2.22">
Let $N \trianglelefteq G$ and $M \trianglelefteq G$ with $N \leq M$. Then $M/N \trianglelefteq G/N$ and

$$(G/N)/(M/N) \cong G/M.$$
</Theorem>

<Proof>
Define $\varphi : G/N \to G/M$ by $\varphi(gN) = gM$. This is well-defined since $N \leq M$: if $gN = g'N$, then $g^{-1}g' \in N \subseteq M$, so $gM = g'M$.

$\varphi$ is a surjective homomorphism. Its kernel is $\ker(\varphi) = \{gN \mid gM = M\} = \{gN \mid g \in M\} = M/N$.

By the first isomorphism theorem: $(G/N)/(M/N) \cong G/M$.
</Proof>

<Remark title="Cancellation of quotients">
The third isomorphism theorem is often remembered as "you can cancel the $N$": $(G/N)/(M/N) \cong G/M$, as if dividing fractions.
</Remark>

<Example id="third-iso-z" title="Third isomorphism theorem in Z">
$G = \mathbb{Z}$, $N = 6\mathbb{Z}$, $M = 2\mathbb{Z}$. Then:

$$(\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z})/(2\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}.$$

Indeed, $2\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} = \{\bar{0}, \bar{2}, \bar{4}\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$, and $(\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z})/(\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z})$ has order $6/3 = 2$.
</Example>

<Example id="third-iso-z12" title="Third isomorphism theorem: Z/12Z">
$G = \mathbb{Z}$, $N = 12\mathbb{Z}$, $M = 4\mathbb{Z}$. Then:

$$(\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z})/(4\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}.$$

Here $4\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} = \{\bar{0}, \bar{4}, \bar{8}\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$, and the quotient $(\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z})/(\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z})$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

<Example id="sn-an" title="Application to Sₙ">
$A_n \trianglelefteq S_n$ and $\{e\} \trianglelefteq S_n$ with $\{e\} \leq A_n$. The third isomorphism theorem gives:

$$(S_n/\{e\})/(A_n/\{e\}) \cong S_n/A_n,$$

which is $S_n/A_n \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ — a tautology, but it illustrates the theorem.
</Example>

<Example id="second-iso-dn" title="Second isomorphism theorem in D_n">
In $D_n$ with $n$ even, let $N = \langle r \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ (rotations) and $H = \{e, s, r^{n/2}, r^{n/2}s\}$ (a Klein four-subgroup when $n \geq 4$). Then $H \cap N = \{e, r^{n/2}\}$ and $HN = D_n$. The second isomorphism theorem gives:

$$H/(H \cap N) \cong HN/N = D_n/\langle r \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}.$$

Since $|H| = 4$ and $|H \cap N| = 2$, indeed $|H/(H \cap N)| = 2$. Check.
</Example>

## Summary of all three isomorphism theorems

<Remark title="Overview">
| Theorem | Setup | Conclusion |
|---------|-------|------------|
| **First** | $\varphi : G \to H$ homomorphism | $G/\ker(\varphi) \cong \operatorname{im}(\varphi)$ |
| **Second** | $H \leq G$, $N \trianglelefteq G$ | $H/(H \cap N) \cong HN/N$ |
| **Third** | $N \leq M$, both $\trianglelefteq G$ | $(G/N)/(M/N) \cong G/M$ |

All three can be proved using the first isomorphism theorem as the core tool: the second and third are obtained by cleverly choosing the right homomorphism and applying the first.
</Remark>
