# Direct Products and Semidirect Products

Direct products build new groups from old ones by taking the Cartesian product. Semidirect products generalize this by allowing a "twist" in how the factors interact.

---

## External direct product

<Definition title="External direct product" number="2.20">
Let $G_1, G_2, \ldots, G_n$ be groups. Their **(external) direct product** is the Cartesian product

$$G_1 \times G_2 \times \cdots \times G_n = \{(g_1, g_2, \ldots, g_n) \mid g_i \in G_i\}$$

with componentwise multiplication:

$$(g_1, \ldots, g_n)(h_1, \ldots, h_n) = (g_1 h_1, \ldots, g_n h_n).$$

The identity is $(e_1, \ldots, e_n)$ and the inverse is $(g_1^{-1}, \ldots, g_n^{-1})$.
</Definition>

<Example id="z2-z3" title="Z/2Z x Z/3Z">
$\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ has 6 elements. The element $(\bar{1}, \bar{1})$ has order $\mathrm{lcm}(2, 3) = 6$, so $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$. This is cyclic because $\gcd(2, 3) = 1$.
</Example>

<Example id="z2-z2" title="Z/2Z x Z/2Z (Klein four-group)">
$\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} = \{(\bar{0},\bar{0}), (\bar{1},\bar{0}), (\bar{0},\bar{1}), (\bar{1},\bar{1})\}$ has 4 elements, each of order $\leq 2$. This is the **Klein four-group** $V_4$. It is **not** cyclic (no element has order 4), so $V_4 \not\cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

<Theorem title="Order in a direct product" number="2.15">
The order of $(g_1, \ldots, g_n)$ in $G_1 \times \cdots \times G_n$ is $\mathrm{lcm}(|g_1|, \ldots, |g_n|)$.
</Theorem>

<Example id="order-product" title="Computing orders in Z/4Z x Z/6Z">
In $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$:

| Element | Order |
|---------|-------|
| $(\bar{1}, \bar{1})$ | $\mathrm{lcm}(4, 6) = 12$ |
| $(\bar{2}, \bar{3})$ | $\mathrm{lcm}(2, 2) = 2$ |
| $(\bar{1}, \bar{2})$ | $\mathrm{lcm}(4, 3) = 12$ |
| $(\bar{0}, \bar{1})$ | $\mathrm{lcm}(1, 6) = 6$ |

Since there is an element of order $12 = |\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}|/2$... actually $|\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}| = 24$ and the maximum order is 12, so this group is **not** cyclic.
</Example>

<Theorem title="When is the direct product cyclic?" number="2.16">
$\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is cyclic if and only if $\gcd(m, n) = 1$, in which case $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/mn\mathbb{Z}$.
</Theorem>

<Example id="cyclic-criterion" title="Cyclic or not?">
- $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/15\mathbb{Z}$ — cyclic, since $\gcd(3,5)=1$.
- $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$ — not cyclic, since $\gcd(2,4)=2$.
- $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/35\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/210\mathbb{Z}$ — cyclic, since $\gcd(6,35)=1$.
</Example>

## Internal direct product

<Definition title="Internal direct product" number="2.21">
A group $G$ is the **(internal) direct product** of normal subgroups $H, K \trianglelefteq G$ if:

1. $G = HK = \{hk \mid h \in H, k \in K\}$,
2. $H \cap K = \{e\}$.

We write $G = H \times K$ (by a slight abuse of notation).
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Internal = External" number="2.17">
If $G = H \times K$ internally, then the map $(h, k) \mapsto hk$ is an isomorphism $H \times K \xrightarrow{\sim} G$ (external to internal). The conditions $H, K \trianglelefteq G$ and $H \cap K = \{e\}$ ensure that elements of $H$ and $K$ commute: $hk = kh$ for all $h \in H$, $k \in K$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
For $h \in H$ and $k \in K$: consider $hkh^{-1}k^{-1}$. Since $K \trianglelefteq G$, $hkh^{-1} \in K$, so $hkh^{-1}k^{-1} \in K$. Since $H \trianglelefteq G$, $kh^{-1}k^{-1} \in H$, so $hkh^{-1}k^{-1} \in H$. Thus $hkh^{-1}k^{-1} \in H \cap K = \{e\}$, giving $hk = kh$.
</Proof>

<Example id="internal-v4" title="V₄ as internal direct product">
In $V_4 = \{e, a, b, ab\}$ (Klein four-group): $H = \{e, a\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ and $K = \{e, b\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ are both normal (since $V_4$ is abelian), $H \cap K = \{e\}$, and $HK = V_4$. So $V_4 = H \times K \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

<Example id="z6-internal" title="Z/6Z as internal direct product">
In $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$: $H = \{\bar{0}, \bar{3}\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ and $K = \{\bar{0}, \bar{2}, \bar{4}\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$. Then $H + K = \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$, $H \cap K = \{\bar{0}\}$. So $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ internally.
</Example>

<Example id="not-direct" title="S₃ is NOT a direct product">
$S_3$ has normal subgroup $A_3 = \{e, (123), (132)\}$ and subgroup $H = \{e, (12)\}$, with $A_3 \cap H = \{e\}$ and $A_3 H = S_3$. But $H$ is **not** normal in $S_3$! So $S_3$ is **not** an internal direct product of $A_3$ and $H$. (It is, however, a semidirect product.)
</Example>

## Semidirect product

<Definition title="Semidirect product" number="2.22">
Let $N \trianglelefteq G$ and $H \leq G$ such that $G = NH$ and $N \cap H = \{e\}$. Then $G$ is a **(internal) semidirect product** of $N$ by $H$, written $G = N \rtimes H$.

Unlike the direct product, we only require $N$ to be normal, not $H$.
</Definition>

<Remark title="The action determines the semidirect product">
In $G = N \rtimes H$, conjugation gives an action of $H$ on $N$: $\varphi : H \to \mathrm{Aut}(N)$ defined by $\varphi(h)(n) = hnh^{-1}$. The group operation is

$$(n_1 h_1)(n_2 h_2) = n_1 (h_1 n_2 h_1^{-1}) h_1 h_2 = (n_1 \cdot \varphi(h_1)(n_2)) \cdot (h_1 h_2).$$

When $\varphi$ is trivial (every $h$ acts as the identity), this reduces to the direct product.
</Remark>

<Definition title="External semidirect product" number="2.23">
Given groups $N$ and $H$ and a homomorphism $\varphi : H \to \mathrm{Aut}(N)$, the **(external) semidirect product** $N \rtimes_\varphi H$ is the set $N \times H$ with multiplication

$$(n_1, h_1)(n_2, h_2) = (n_1 \cdot \varphi(h_1)(n_2),\; h_1 h_2).$$
</Definition>

<Example id="s3-semidirect" title="S₃ as a semidirect product">
$S_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \rtimes \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, where $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ acts on $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ by inversion: $\varphi(\bar{1})(\bar{k}) = -\bar{k}$. Explicitly, with $N = A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ and $H = \{e, (12)\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$:

$$(12) \cdot (123) \cdot (12)^{-1} = (132) = (123)^{-1}.$$

So the transposition acts on $A_3$ by inversion, matching the semidirect product structure.
</Example>

<Example id="dn-semidirect" title="Dihedral group as semidirect product">
$D_n \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \rtimes \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, where the generator of $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ acts on $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ by $\bar{k} \mapsto -\bar{k}$ (inversion). The rotation subgroup $\langle r \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is normal, and a reflection $s$ generates a complement $\{e, s\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ with $srs^{-1} = r^{-1}$.
</Example>

<Example id="trivial-semidirect" title="Trivial action gives direct product">
If $\varphi : H \to \mathrm{Aut}(N)$ is the trivial map ($\varphi(h) = \mathrm{id}_N$ for all $h$), then $N \rtimes_\varphi H = N \times H$. The semidirect product generalizes the direct product.
</Example>

<Example id="semidirect-z-z" title="Z/n x Z/2 vs Z/n ⋊ Z/2">
With $N = \mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z}$ and $H = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$:

- **Trivial action**: $\mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z}$ (cyclic, abelian).
- **Inversion action** ($\bar{1} \mapsto (\bar{k} \mapsto -\bar{k})$): $\mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z} \rtimes \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \cong D_5$ (non-abelian, dihedral of order 10).

Different actions give genuinely different groups.
</Example>

## Recognizing direct and semidirect products

<Theorem title="Direct product recognition" number="2.18">
$G \cong H \times K$ if and only if there exist $H', K' \trianglelefteq G$ with $G = H'K'$, $H' \cap K' = \{e\}$, $H' \cong H$, and $K' \cong K$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem title="Semidirect product recognition" number="2.19">
$G \cong N \rtimes H$ if and only if there exist $N' \trianglelefteq G$ and $H' \leq G$ with $G = N'H'$, $N' \cap H' = \{e\}$, $N' \cong N$, and $H' \cong H$.

The subgroup $H'$ is called a **complement** to $N'$.
</Theorem>

<Example id="ga-semidirect" title="Affine group as semidirect product">
The **affine group** $\mathrm{Aff}(F) = \{x \mapsto ax + b \mid a \in F^*, b \in F\}$ is a semidirect product:

$$\mathrm{Aff}(F) \cong F \rtimes F^*,$$

where $F^*$ acts on the additive group $(F, +)$ by multiplication: $\varphi(a)(b) = ab$. The translations $\{x \mapsto x + b\} \cong (F, +)$ form the normal subgroup, and the scalings $\{x \mapsto ax\} \cong F^*$ form the complement.
</Example>

<Example id="classify-order6" title="Groups of order 6">
Every group of order 6 is either $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ or $S_3$. Both can be written in terms of products:

- $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ (direct product).
- $S_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \rtimes \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ (semidirect product with nontrivial action).

The unique element of $\mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ of order 2 is inversion, so there are exactly two semidirect products, giving exactly two groups.
</Example>

## Summary

<Remark title="Key takeaways">
1. **Direct product** $G = H \times K$: both $H, K \trianglelefteq G$, $H \cap K = \{e\}$, $G = HK$, and elements of $H$ and $K$ commute.
2. **Semidirect product** $G = N \rtimes H$: only $N$ need be normal. The group structure is determined by the action $\varphi : H \to \mathrm{Aut}(N)$.
3. $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/mn\mathbb{Z}$ iff $\gcd(m,n) = 1$.
4. Semidirect products are the main tool for constructing and classifying non-abelian groups of small order.
</Remark>
