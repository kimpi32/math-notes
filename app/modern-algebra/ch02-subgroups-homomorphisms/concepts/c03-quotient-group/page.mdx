# Quotient Groups

When $N$ is a normal subgroup of $G$, the set of cosets $G/N$ inherits a natural group structure. This construction is fundamental to group theory and connects directly to the isomorphism theorems.

---

## Coset multiplication

<Definition title="Quotient group" number="2.13">
Let $N \trianglelefteq G$. The **quotient group** (or **factor group**) $G/N$ is the set of left cosets $\{gN \mid g \in G\}$ equipped with the operation

$$(aN)(bN) = (ab)N.$$

The identity element is $eN = N$, and the inverse of $aN$ is $a^{-1}N$.
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Well-definedness of coset multiplication" number="2.5">
Let $N \trianglelefteq G$. The coset multiplication $(aN)(bN) = (ab)N$ is well-defined: if $aN = a'N$ and $bN = b'N$, then $(ab)N = (a'b')N$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
Suppose $a' = an_1$ and $b' = bn_2$ for $n_1, n_2 \in N$. Then

$$a'b' = an_1 \cdot bn_2 = a \cdot (n_1 b) \cdot n_2.$$

Since $N$ is normal, $n_1 b = b(b^{-1}n_1 b) = b n_3$ for some $n_3 \in N$. So $a'b' = ab \cdot n_3 n_2$, which means $a'b' \in (ab)N$, i.e., $(a'b')N = (ab)N$.
</Proof>

<Remark title="Normality is necessary">
If $H$ is **not** normal, coset multiplication is not well-defined. For example, in $S_3$ with $H = \{e, (12)\}$: the coset $(13)H = \{(13), (132)\}$ and $(23)H = \{(23), (123)\}$. If we try $(13)H \cdot (23)H = (13)(23)H = (132)H = (13)H$. But picking different representatives: $(132) \cdot (123) = e$, so $(132)H \cdot (123)H = eH = H \neq (13)H$. Contradiction.
</Remark>

## The canonical projection

<Definition title="Canonical projection" number="2.14">
The **canonical projection** (or **quotient map**) is the surjective homomorphism

$$\pi : G \to G/N, \quad \pi(g) = gN.$$

Its kernel is $\ker(\pi) = N$.
</Definition>

<Remark title="Every normal subgroup is a kernel">
Combining this with the fact that every kernel is normal, we get: **a subgroup $N \leq G$ is normal if and only if $N = \ker(\varphi)$ for some homomorphism $\varphi$ from $G$**.
</Remark>

## Examples

<Example id="z-mod-n" title="Z/nZ as a quotient group">
$n\mathbb{Z} \trianglelefteq \mathbb{Z}$, and the quotient $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ has elements $\bar{0}, \bar{1}, \ldots, \overline{n-1}$ with addition modulo $n$. This is the cyclic group of order $n$.

The canonical projection $\pi : \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ sends $k \mapsto \bar{k} = k \bmod n$.
</Example>

<Example id="gl-sl" title="GL(n,F)/SL(n,F)">
$\mathrm{SL}(n, F) \trianglelefteq \mathrm{GL}(n, F)$, and the determinant map $\det : \mathrm{GL}(n, F) \to F^*$ has kernel $\mathrm{SL}(n, F)$. By the first isomorphism theorem,

$$\mathrm{GL}(n, F) / \mathrm{SL}(n, F) \cong F^*.$$

Two matrices are in the same coset iff they have the same determinant.
</Example>

<Example id="s3-a3" title="S₃/A₃">
$A_3 \trianglelefteq S_3$ with $[S_3 : A_3] = 2$. The two cosets are $A_3$ (even permutations) and $(12)A_3$ (odd permutations). Thus

$$S_3/A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}.$$

The quotient map is the sign homomorphism $\mathrm{sgn} : S_3 \to \{+1, -1\}$.
</Example>

<Example id="r-z" title="R/Z is the circle group">
$\mathbb{Z} \trianglelefteq (\mathbb{R}, +)$, and the quotient $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$ can be identified with the **circle group** $S^1 = \{e^{2\pi i \theta} \mid \theta \in [0,1)\}$ via the isomorphism $\theta + \mathbb{Z} \mapsto e^{2\pi i \theta}$.
</Example>

<Example id="z-nz-mz" title="Quotient of Z/12Z">
$\langle \bar{4} \rangle = \{\bar{0}, \bar{4}, \bar{8}\} \trianglelefteq \mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$ (normal since $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$ is abelian). The quotient

$$(\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}) / \langle \bar{4} \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$$

has 4 cosets: $\bar{0} + \langle \bar{4} \rangle$, $\bar{1} + \langle \bar{4} \rangle$, $\bar{2} + \langle \bar{4} \rangle$, $\bar{3} + \langle \bar{4} \rangle$.
</Example>

<Example id="quotient-d4" title="Quotient of D₄ by its center">
The dihedral group $D_4$ of order 8 has center $Z(D_4) = \{e, r^2\}$, where $r$ is rotation by $90°$. The quotient

$$D_4 / Z(D_4)$$

has order $8/2 = 4$. Since it is generated by the images of $r$ and $s$ (a reflection) with $\bar{r}^2 = \bar{e}$ and $\bar{s}^2 = \bar{e}$,

$$D_4/Z(D_4) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$$

(the Klein four-group), not $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

## Properties of quotient groups

<Theorem title="Order of the quotient" number="2.6">
If $G$ is finite and $N \trianglelefteq G$, then $|G/N| = |G|/|N| = [G : N]$.
</Theorem>

<Example id="order-quotient" title="Order computation">
$|\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}| = 6$, $|2\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}| = |\{\bar{0}, \bar{2}, \bar{4}\}| = 3$, so $|(\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}) / (2\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z})| = 6/3 = 2$.
</Example>

<Theorem title="Quotient of cyclic is cyclic" number="2.7">
If $G$ is cyclic and $N \trianglelefteq G$, then $G/N$ is cyclic. If $G = \langle a \rangle$, then $G/N = \langle aN \rangle$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem title="Quotient of abelian is abelian" number="2.8">
If $G$ is abelian and $N \trianglelefteq G$, then $G/N$ is abelian.
</Theorem>

<Example id="quotient-abelian" title="Quotients preserve commutativity">
$(aN)(bN) = (ab)N = (ba)N = (bN)(aN)$ when $G$ is abelian. More interesting: $G/N$ can be abelian even if $G$ is not. For instance, $S_3/A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ is abelian, but $S_3$ is not.
</Example>

## Quotient by the center and inner automorphisms

<Definition title="Inner automorphism" number="2.15">
For $g \in G$, the map $\varphi_g : G \to G$ defined by $\varphi_g(x) = gxg^{-1}$ is an automorphism of $G$ called an **inner automorphism**. The group of all inner automorphisms is

$$\mathrm{Inn}(G) = \{\varphi_g \mid g \in G\} \leq \mathrm{Aut}(G).$$
</Definition>

<Theorem title="G/Z(G) is isomorphic to Inn(G)" number="2.9">
The map $G \to \mathrm{Inn}(G)$ defined by $g \mapsto \varphi_g$ is a surjective homomorphism with kernel $Z(G)$. Therefore

$$G/Z(G) \cong \mathrm{Inn}(G).$$
</Theorem>

<Example id="inn-s3" title="Inner automorphisms of S₃">
$Z(S_3) = \{e\}$, so $S_3/Z(S_3) \cong S_3 \cong \mathrm{Inn}(S_3)$. Every automorphism of $S_3$ is inner (in fact, $\mathrm{Aut}(S_3) \cong S_3$).
</Example>

<Example id="inn-d4" title="Inn(D₄)">
$Z(D_4) = \{e, r^2\}$, so $\mathrm{Inn}(D_4) \cong D_4/Z(D_4) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

## The quotient is "universal"

<Remark title="Universal property of the quotient">
If $\varphi : G \to H$ is a homomorphism with $N \subseteq \ker(\varphi)$, then there exists a unique homomorphism $\bar{\varphi} : G/N \to H$ such that $\bar{\varphi} \circ \pi = \varphi$, where $\pi : G \to G/N$ is the projection. Explicitly, $\bar{\varphi}(gN) = \varphi(g)$.

This universal property says: **to map out of $G/N$, it suffices to give a map out of $G$ that kills $N$.**
</Remark>

<Example id="universal-property" title="Application: factoring Z to Z/3Z through Z/6Z">
The map $\varphi : \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$, $n \mapsto n \bmod 3$, kills $6\mathbb{Z}$ (since $6\mathbb{Z} \subseteq 3\mathbb{Z} = \ker(\varphi)$). So it factors through $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$:

$$\bar{\varphi} : \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}, \quad \bar{k} \mapsto k \bmod 3.$$

This is a well-defined surjective homomorphism with kernel $\{\bar{0}, \bar{3}\} = \langle \bar{3} \rangle$, giving $(\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z})/\langle \bar{3} \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

<Example id="quotient-q8" title="Quotient of the quaternion group">
The quaternion group $Q_8 = \{\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\}$ has center $Z(Q_8) = \{1, -1\}$. The quotient $Q_8/Z(Q_8)$ has order 4. Since $\bar{i}^2 = \overline{i^2} = \overline{-1} = \bar{e}$ and similarly $\bar{j}^2 = \bar{e}$, every non-identity element has order 2, so

$$Q_8/Z(Q_8) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$$

(the Klein four-group). Note that $Q_8$ is non-abelian, but the quotient by its center is abelian.
</Example>

<Example id="z-times-z-quotient" title="Quotient of Z x Z">
$\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}$ has normal subgroup $N = \{(n, 0) \mid n \in \mathbb{Z}\} \cong \mathbb{Z}$. The quotient

$$(\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z})/N \cong \mathbb{Z},$$

via the map $(a, b) + N \mapsto b$. This is a simple instance of "quotienting out one factor of a direct product yields the other."
</Example>

## Summary

<Remark title="Key takeaways">
1. $G/N$ is a group iff $N \trianglelefteq G$; normality is **precisely** the condition for coset multiplication to be well-defined.
2. $|G/N| = [G:N]$. Quotients of cyclic/abelian groups remain cyclic/abelian.
3. The canonical projection $\pi : G \to G/N$ is a surjective homomorphism with $\ker(\pi) = N$.
4. $G/Z(G) \cong \mathrm{Inn}(G)$.
5. The quotient satisfies a universal property: every homomorphism killing $N$ factors uniquely through $G/N$.
</Remark>
