# Groups and Basic Properties

A **group** is one of the most fundamental algebraic structures in mathematics. It captures the abstract essence of symmetry and provides the language for studying transformations, equations, and invariants across all of mathematics.

---

## Definition

<Definition title="Group" number="1.1">
A **group** is a pair $(G, \cdot)$ where $G$ is a non-empty set and $\cdot : G \times G \to G$ is a binary operation satisfying the following three axioms:

1. **Associativity.** For all $a, b, c \in G$:

$$a \cdot (b \cdot c) = (a \cdot b) \cdot c$$

2. **Identity element.** There exists an element $e \in G$ such that for all $a \in G$:

$$e \cdot a = a \cdot e = a$$

3. **Inverse element.** For each $a \in G$, there exists $a^{-1} \in G$ such that:

$$a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = e$$
</Definition>

<Remark title="Notation conventions">
We often write $ab$ instead of $a \cdot b$ when the operation is clear. For additive groups (typically abelian), we write $a + b$, denote the identity by $0$, and the inverse of $a$ by $-a$.
</Remark>

---

## Uniqueness Results

<Theorem title="Uniqueness of identity" number="1.1">
The identity element $e$ in a group $G$ is **unique**.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
Suppose $e$ and $e'$ are both identity elements. Then:

$$e = e \cdot e' = e'$$

where the first equality uses the identity property of $e'$ and the second uses that of $e$.
</Proof>

<Theorem title="Uniqueness of inverses" number="1.2">
For each $a \in G$, the inverse $a^{-1}$ is **unique**.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
Suppose $b$ and $c$ are both inverses of $a$. Then:

$$b = b \cdot e = b \cdot (a \cdot c) = (b \cdot a) \cdot c = e \cdot c = c$$
</Proof>

## Basic Properties

<Theorem title="Cancellation laws" number="1.3">
In any group $G$, for all $a, b, c \in G$:

- **Left cancellation:** If $a \cdot b = a \cdot c$, then $b = c$.
- **Right cancellation:** If $b \cdot a = c \cdot a$, then $b = c$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
For left cancellation: multiply both sides on the left by $a^{-1}$:

$$a^{-1}(ab) = a^{-1}(ac) \implies (a^{-1}a)b = (a^{-1}a)c \implies eb = ec \implies b = c$$

Right cancellation is analogous.
</Proof>

<Theorem title="Inverse of a product" number="1.4">
For all $a, b \in G$:

$$(ab)^{-1} = b^{-1}a^{-1}$$

Note the reversal of order (the "socks-shoes" property).
</Theorem>

<Proof>
We verify directly:

$$(ab)(b^{-1}a^{-1}) = a(bb^{-1})a^{-1} = aea^{-1} = aa^{-1} = e$$

By uniqueness of inverses, $(ab)^{-1} = b^{-1}a^{-1}$.
</Proof>

<Theorem title="Inverse of an inverse" number="1.5">
For all $a \in G$: $(a^{-1})^{-1} = a$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
Since $a^{-1} \cdot a = e$, the element $a$ is an inverse of $a^{-1}$. By uniqueness, $(a^{-1})^{-1} = a$.
</Proof>

## The Order of a Group

<Definition title="Order of a group" number="1.2">
The **order** of a group $G$, denoted $|G|$, is the cardinality of the underlying set $G$. If $|G|$ is finite, $G$ is called a **finite group**; otherwise, an **infinite group**.
</Definition>

## Examples of Groups

<Example id="integers" title="The integers under addition">
$(\mathbb{Z}, +)$ is a group:

- **Associativity:** $(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)$ for all integers.
- **Identity:** $0$, since $a + 0 = a$.
- **Inverses:** $-a$ is the inverse of $a$.

This is an infinite group.
</Example>

<Example id="rationals-mult" title="Non-zero rationals under multiplication">
$(\mathbb{Q}^*, \cdot)$ is a group, where $\mathbb{Q}^* = \mathbb{Q} \setminus \{0\}$:

- **Associativity:** Multiplication of rationals is associative.
- **Identity:** $1$.
- **Inverses:** $a^{-1} = 1/a$ for $a \neq 0$.

Similarly, $(\mathbb{R}^*, \cdot)$ and $(\mathbb{C}^*, \cdot)$ are groups.
</Example>

<Example id="trivial" title="The trivial group">
$G = \{e\}$ with $e \cdot e = e$ is a group of order 1, called the **trivial group**. It is the unique group of order 1.
</Example>

<Example id="z-mod-n" title="Integers modulo n">
$\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} = \{0, 1, 2, \ldots, n-1\}$ with addition modulo $n$ is a group of order $n$.

For $n = 4$: $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} = \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$, with $2 + 3 = 1$ (since $5 \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$).
</Example>

<Example id="units-mod-n" title="Units modulo n">
$(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times = \{a \in \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} : \gcd(a, n) = 1\}$ under multiplication modulo $n$ is a group.

For $n = 8$: $(\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z})^\times = \{1, 3, 5, 7\}$, with $|(\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z})^\times| = \varphi(8) = 4$ where $\varphi$ is Euler's totient function.

The multiplication table:

| $\cdot$ | 1 | 3 | 5 | 7 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1 | 3 | 5 | 7 |
| 3 | 3 | 1 | 7 | 5 |
| 5 | 5 | 7 | 1 | 3 |
| 7 | 7 | 5 | 3 | 1 |

Every element is its own inverse: $3^2 = 9 \equiv 1$, $5^2 = 25 \equiv 1$, $7^2 = 49 \equiv 1$.
</Example>

<Example id="gl-n" title="General linear group">
$\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{R}) = \{A \in M_{n \times n}(\mathbb{R}) : \det(A) \neq 0\}$ under matrix multiplication is a group.

- **Associativity:** Matrix multiplication is associative.
- **Identity:** The identity matrix $I_n$.
- **Inverses:** Since $\det(A) \neq 0$, the matrix $A$ is invertible.

This is an infinite, non-abelian group for $n \geq 2$. Its order is uncountable.
</Example>

<Example id="sl-n" title="Special linear group">
$\mathrm{SL}_n(\mathbb{R}) = \{A \in M_{n \times n}(\mathbb{R}) : \det(A) = 1\}$ is a group under matrix multiplication. It is a subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$, since $\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$ and $\det(I_n) = 1$.
</Example>

<Example id="roots-unity" title="Roots of unity">
The $n$th roots of unity $\mu_n = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : z^n = 1\} = \{e^{2\pi i k/n} : k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1\}$ form a group of order $n$ under multiplication.

For $n = 4$: $\mu_4 = \{1, i, -1, -i\}$, with $i \cdot i = -1$, $i \cdot (-1) = -i$, etc.
</Example>

<Example id="klein-four" title="The Klein four-group">
$V_4 = \{e, a, b, c\}$ with the multiplication table:

| $\cdot$ | $e$ | $a$ | $b$ | $c$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $e$ | $e$ | $a$ | $b$ | $c$ |
| $a$ | $a$ | $e$ | $c$ | $b$ |
| $b$ | $b$ | $c$ | $e$ | $a$ |
| $c$ | $c$ | $b$ | $a$ | $e$ |

Every non-identity element has order 2. This group is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

<Example id="symmetries-rect" title="Symmetries of a rectangle">
The symmetry group of a (non-square) rectangle has 4 elements: the identity, rotation by $180°$, horizontal reflection, and vertical reflection. This group is isomorphic to the Klein four-group $V_4$.
</Example>

<Example id="matrix-non-abelian" title="Matrices: non-commutativity">
In $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$, consider the matrices $A$ and $B$ where:

- $A$ has rows $(1, 1)$ and $(0, 1)$ (an upper triangular shear)
- $B$ has rows $(0, 1)$ and $(1, 0)$ (the permutation matrix swapping coordinates)

Then $AB$ has rows $(1, 1)$ and $(1, 0)$, while $BA$ has rows $(0, 1)$ and $(1, 1)$.

Since $AB \neq BA$, the group $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ is **not abelian**.
</Example>

<Example id="non-group" title="Non-examples: why groups need all axioms">
Some structures fail to be groups:

- $(\mathbb{Z}, \cdot)$: multiplication is associative, $1$ is an identity, but $2$ has no multiplicative inverse in $\mathbb{Z}$. Not a group.
- $(\mathbb{N}, +)$: no identity (if we exclude 0), or no inverses. Not a group.
- $(M_{2 \times 2}(\mathbb{R}), \cdot)$: the zero matrix has no inverse. Not a group (but $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ is).
</Example>

## Subgroups

<Definition title="Subgroup" number="1.3">
A subset $H \subseteq G$ is a **subgroup** of $G$, written $H \leq G$, if $H$ is itself a group under the operation inherited from $G$.
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Subgroup criterion" number="1.6">
A non-empty subset $H \subseteq G$ is a subgroup if and only if for all $a, b \in H$:

$$ab^{-1} \in H.$$
</Theorem>

<Proof>
$(\Rightarrow)$ If $H$ is a subgroup, then $b^{-1} \in H$ and hence $ab^{-1} \in H$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Assume $H$ is non-empty and closed under $ab^{-1}$. Pick any $a \in H$; then $e = aa^{-1} \in H$. For any $b \in H$, $b^{-1} = eb^{-1} \in H$. For any $a, b \in H$, $ab = a(b^{-1})^{-1} \in H$. Associativity is inherited from $G$.
</Proof>

<Example id="subgroup-examples" title="Basic subgroups">
- $\{e\}$ and $G$ are always subgroups (the **trivial** and **improper** subgroups).
- $n\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -2n, -n, 0, n, 2n, \ldots\}$ is a subgroup of $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$ for any $n \geq 0$.
- $\mathrm{SL}_n(\mathbb{R}) \leq \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$.
- $\mu_n \leq \mathbb{C}^*$ (roots of unity inside the multiplicative group of complex numbers).
</Example>

## Powers and Exponentiation

<Definition title="Powers of an element" number="1.4">
For $g \in G$ and $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, define:

- $g^0 = e$
- $g^n = g \cdot g \cdots g$ ($n$ times) for $n > 0$
- $g^{-n} = (g^{-1})^n$ for $n > 0$

The usual **laws of exponents** hold: $g^{m+n} = g^m g^n$ and $(g^m)^n = g^{mn}$.
</Definition>

<Remark title="Additive notation">
In additive notation, we write $ng = g + g + \cdots + g$ ($n$ times) instead of $g^n$.
</Remark>

---

## Summary

Groups formalize the notion of "reversible operations" on a set. The key properties — uniqueness of identity and inverses, cancellation laws, and the socks-shoes rule for inverse products — follow purely from the three group axioms. The examples above show that groups arise naturally in number theory, linear algebra, geometry, and combinatorics.
