# Galois Groups

The **Galois group** of a field extension captures the symmetries of the roots of a polynomial. Evariste Galois's revolutionary insight was that the solvability of a polynomial equation is governed by the group-theoretic structure of these symmetries.

---

## Definition and basic properties

<Definition title="Automorphism group of an extension" number="5.15">
Let $E/F$ be a field extension. The **automorphism group** of $E/F$ is

$$\operatorname{Aut}(E/F) = \{\sigma : E \to E \mid \sigma \text{ is a field automorphism with } \sigma|_F = \operatorname{id}_F\}.$$

This is a group under composition.
</Definition>

<Definition title="Galois extension and Galois group" number="5.16">
A finite extension $E/F$ is a **Galois extension** if $|\operatorname{Aut}(E/F)| = [E:F]$.

When $E/F$ is Galois, we write $\operatorname{Gal}(E/F) = \operatorname{Aut}(E/F)$ and call it the **Galois group** of $E/F$.
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Characterization of Galois extensions" number="5.9">
For a finite extension $E/F$, the following are equivalent:
1. $E/F$ is Galois.
2. $E$ is the splitting field of a separable polynomial in $F[x]$.
3. $E/F$ is both normal and separable.
4. $F = E^{\operatorname{Aut}(E/F)}$ (the fixed field of $\operatorname{Aut}(E/F)$ is exactly $F$).
</Theorem>

<Remark title="Inequality in general">
For any finite extension $E/F$, we always have $|\operatorname{Aut}(E/F)| \le [E:F]$. Equality (the Galois condition) fails precisely when the extension is not normal or not separable.
</Remark>

## First examples

<Example id="gal-qsqrt2" title="Gal(Q(sqrt(2))/Q)">
$E = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ is the splitting field of $x^2 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, so $E/\mathbb{Q}$ is Galois. Any $\sigma \in \operatorname{Gal}(E/\mathbb{Q})$ must send $\sqrt{2}$ to a root of $x^2 - 2$, so $\sigma(\sqrt{2}) = \pm\sqrt{2}$. Thus:

$$\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})/\mathbb{Q}) = \{\operatorname{id}, \sigma\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$$

where $\sigma(a + b\sqrt{2}) = a - b\sqrt{2}$.
</Example>

<Example id="gal-qi" title="Gal(Q(i)/Q)">
$\mathbb{Q}(i)$ is the splitting field of $x^2 + 1$. The nontrivial automorphism sends $i \mapsto -i$ (complex conjugation restricted to $\mathbb{Q}(i)$). So $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(i)/\mathbb{Q}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.
</Example>

<Example id="gal-cr" title="Gal(C/R)">
$\mathbb{C} = \mathbb{R}(i)$ is the splitting field of $x^2 + 1$ over $\mathbb{R}$. Complex conjugation $z \mapsto \bar{z}$ is the unique nontrivial automorphism:

$$\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{C}/\mathbb{R}) = \{\operatorname{id}, \text{conjugation}\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}.$$
</Example>

<Example id="not-galois" title="A non-Galois extension">
$\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})/\mathbb{Q}$ has degree $3$, but $|\operatorname{Aut}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})/\mathbb{Q})| = 1$. Any automorphism $\sigma$ must send $\sqrt[3]{2}$ to a root of $x^3 - 2$, but the other roots $\omega\sqrt[3]{2}$ and $\omega^2\sqrt[3]{2}$ are complex, and $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}) \subset \mathbb{R}$. So $\sigma(\sqrt[3]{2}) = \sqrt[3]{2}$, and $\sigma = \operatorname{id}$.

This extension is separable but not normal.
</Example>

## Galois groups of splitting fields

<Example id="gal-x3-2" title="Gal of the splitting field of x^3 - 2">
Let $E = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$ be the splitting field of $x^3 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, with $[E:\mathbb{Q}] = 6$. The roots are $\alpha = \sqrt[3]{2}$, $\omega\alpha$, $\omega^2\alpha$.

Any $\sigma \in \operatorname{Gal}(E/\mathbb{Q})$ is determined by $\sigma(\alpha)$ and $\sigma(\omega)$:
- $\sigma(\alpha) \in \{\alpha, \omega\alpha, \omega^2\alpha\}$ (3 choices)
- $\sigma(\omega) \in \{\omega, \omega^2\}$ (2 choices)

This gives $|\operatorname{Gal}(E/\mathbb{Q})| = 6$, so $\operatorname{Gal}(E/\mathbb{Q}) \cong S_3$, the symmetric group on the three roots.
</Example>

<Example id="gal-x4-2" title="Gal of the splitting field of x^4 - 2">
Let $E = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2}, i)$, the splitting field of $x^4 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, with $[E:\mathbb{Q}] = 8$. The roots are $\sqrt[4]{2}, i\sqrt[4]{2}, -\sqrt[4]{2}, -i\sqrt[4]{2}$.

$\operatorname{Gal}(E/\mathbb{Q}) \cong D_4$ (the dihedral group of order $8$), generated by:
- $r: \sqrt[4]{2} \mapsto i\sqrt[4]{2}, \; i \mapsto i$ (a rotation of the roots)
- $s: \sqrt[4]{2} \mapsto \sqrt[4]{2}, \; i \mapsto -i$ (complex conjugation)

with $r^4 = s^2 = 1$ and $srs = r^{-1}$.
</Example>

<Example id="gal-biquadratic" title="Gal of biquadratic extensions">
Let $E = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3})$, the splitting field of $(x^2-2)(x^2-3)$, with $[E:\mathbb{Q}] = 4$. The Galois group has three nontrivial automorphisms:

$$\sigma_1: \sqrt{2} \mapsto -\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3} \mapsto \sqrt{3}$$
$$\sigma_2: \sqrt{2} \mapsto \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3} \mapsto -\sqrt{3}$$
$$\sigma_3: \sqrt{2} \mapsto -\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3} \mapsto -\sqrt{3}$$

Each has order $2$, so $\operatorname{Gal}(E/\mathbb{Q}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ (the Klein four-group $V_4$).
</Example>

## Fixed fields

<Definition title="Fixed field" number="5.17">
For a group $H \le \operatorname{Aut}(E)$ of automorphisms of a field $E$, the **fixed field** of $H$ is

$$E^H = \{a \in E \mid \sigma(a) = a \text{ for all } \sigma \in H\}.$$
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Artin's theorem" number="5.10">
Let $G$ be a finite group of automorphisms of a field $E$. Then $E/E^G$ is a Galois extension with $\operatorname{Gal}(E/E^G) = G$ and $[E : E^G] = |G|$.
</Theorem>

<Example id="fixed-field-conjugation" title="Fixed field of complex conjugation">
Let $G = \{\operatorname{id}, \text{conjugation}\} \le \operatorname{Aut}(\mathbb{C})$. Then $\mathbb{C}^G = \mathbb{R}$: the real numbers are exactly the complex numbers fixed by conjugation.
</Example>

<Example id="fixed-field-s3" title="Fixed fields in Gal(Q(cbrt(2), omega)/Q)">
For $E = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$ with $G = \operatorname{Gal}(E/\mathbb{Q}) \cong S_3$:

- $E^G = \mathbb{Q}$ (the whole group fixes only $\mathbb{Q}$)
- Let $H = \langle s \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ where $s$ is conjugation ($\omega \mapsto \omega^2$, $\sqrt[3]{2} \mapsto \sqrt[3]{2}$). Then $E^H = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$.
- Let $N = \langle r \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ where $r$ cyclically permutes the cube roots. Then $E^N = \mathbb{Q}(\omega)$.
</Example>

## Galois groups and permutations of roots

<Theorem title="Galois group embeds in Sn" number="5.11">
Let $f(x) \in F[x]$ be a separable polynomial of degree $n$ with splitting field $E/F$. Let $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n$ be the roots of $f$ in $E$. Then $\operatorname{Gal}(E/F)$ acts faithfully on $\{\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n\}$ by permutation, giving an embedding

$$\operatorname{Gal}(E/F) \hookrightarrow S_n.$$
</Theorem>

<Example id="gal-as-subgroup-s3" title="Galois group as a subgroup of S_3">
For $f(x) = x^3 - 2$, the Galois group $\operatorname{Gal}(E/\mathbb{Q}) \cong S_3$ embeds as the full symmetric group on $3$ roots. This is the "generic" case for irreducible cubics.

For $f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 1$ (whose roots are $2\cos(2\pi/9), 2\cos(4\pi/9), 2\cos(8\pi/9)$), the Galois group is $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \hookrightarrow S_3$ (cyclic, since the discriminant is a perfect square).
</Example>

<Example id="discriminant-test" title="Discriminant and the alternating group">
For a separable polynomial $f$ of degree $n$, define the **discriminant** $\Delta = \prod_{i < j} (\alpha_i - \alpha_j)^2$. Then:

$$\operatorname{Gal}(E/F) \subseteq A_n \iff \sqrt{\Delta} \in F.$$

For a cubic $x^3 + px + q$, the discriminant is $\Delta = -4p^3 - 27q^2$.
</Example>

## Cyclotomic extensions

<Definition title="Cyclotomic field" number="5.18">
The $n$-th **cyclotomic field** is $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$ where $\zeta_n = e^{2\pi i/n}$ is a primitive $n$-th root of unity. It is the splitting field of the $n$-th cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_n(x)$.
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Galois group of cyclotomic fields" number="5.12">
$\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q}$ is Galois with

$$\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$$

where the isomorphism sends $\sigma_a$ (defined by $\sigma_a(\zeta_n) = \zeta_n^a$) to $a \bmod n$. The degree is $[\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n):\mathbb{Q}] = \varphi(n)$ (Euler's totient).
</Theorem>

<Example id="cyclotomic-5" title="The 5th cyclotomic field">
$\Phi_5(x) = x^4 + x^3 + x^2 + x + 1$. The Galois group $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_5)/\mathbb{Q}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z})^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$, which is cyclic of order $4$, generated by $\sigma_2: \zeta_5 \mapsto \zeta_5^2$.
</Example>

<Example id="cyclotomic-8" title="The 8th cyclotomic field">
$\Phi_8(x) = x^4 + 1$. We have $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_8)/\mathbb{Q}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z})^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ (Klein four-group). Note $\zeta_8 = (1+i)/\sqrt{2}$, and $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_8) = \mathbb{Q}(i, \sqrt{2})$.
</Example>

<Example id="cyclotomic-7" title="The 7th cyclotomic field">
$\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_7)/\mathbb{Q}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/7\mathbb{Z})^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$, cyclic of order $6$. It has subgroups of orders $1, 2, 3, 6$, corresponding to intermediate fields of degrees $6, 3, 2, 1$ over $\mathbb{Q}$.
</Example>

## Galois group computation techniques

<Example id="compute-by-roots" title="Computation by tracking roots">
To compute $\operatorname{Gal}(E/F)$ for the splitting field of $f(x) \in F[x]$:

1. Find the roots $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n$ of $f$.
2. Each $\sigma \in \operatorname{Gal}(E/F)$ permutes the roots.
3. $\sigma$ is determined by its action on a set of generators of $E/F$.
4. Check which permutations actually extend to field automorphisms.

For instance, for $f(x) = x^4 + 1$ over $\mathbb{Q}$: the roots are $\zeta_8, \zeta_8^3, \zeta_8^5, \zeta_8^7$. The Galois group must preserve multiplicative relations, so not every permutation of roots works.
</Example>

<Example id="transitive-action" title="Transitivity and irreducibility">
If $f(x) \in F[x]$ is irreducible of degree $n$, then $\operatorname{Gal}(E/F)$ acts **transitively** on the roots (any root can be sent to any other root). This means $\operatorname{Gal}(E/F)$ is a transitive subgroup of $S_n$, which constrains it significantly.

For $n = 2$: the only transitive subgroup is $S_2 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

For $n = 3$: transitive subgroups are $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ and $S_3$.

For $n = 4$: transitive subgroups are $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$, $V_4$, $D_4$, $A_4$, and $S_4$.
</Example>

## Summary

| Extension | Galois? | Galois group | Order |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})/\mathbb{Q}$ | Yes | $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ | $2$ |
| $\mathbb{Q}(i)/\mathbb{Q}$ | Yes | $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ | $2$ |
| $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})/\mathbb{Q}$ | **No** | $\operatorname{Aut} = \{1\}$ | $1 < 3$ |
| $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2},\omega)/\mathbb{Q}$ | Yes | $S_3$ | $6$ |
| $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2},i)/\mathbb{Q}$ | Yes | $D_4$ | $8$ |
| $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3})/\mathbb{Q}$ | Yes | $V_4$ | $4$ |
| $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p)/\mathbb{Q}$ | Yes | $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$ | $p-1$ |

The [Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory](/modern-algebra/ch05-fields-galois/theorems/t01-fundamental-galois) establishes a bijection between intermediate fields of a Galois extension and subgroups of the Galois group, translating field-theoretic questions into group theory.
</Remark>
