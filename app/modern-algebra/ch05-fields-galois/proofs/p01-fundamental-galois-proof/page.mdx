# Proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory

We give a complete proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory. The proof builds on Artin's theorem (fixed fields), the theory of separable and normal extensions, and the counting of automorphisms.

---

## Setup and notation

Let $E/F$ be a finite Galois extension with $G = \operatorname{Gal}(E/F)$.

We define two maps:

$$\Phi: \{\text{intermediate fields } F \subseteq K \subseteq E\} \to \{\text{subgroups } H \le G\}, \quad \Phi(K) = \operatorname{Gal}(E/K)$$

$$\Psi: \{\text{subgroups } H \le G\} \to \{\text{intermediate fields } F \subseteq K \subseteq E\}, \quad \Psi(H) = E^H$$

We must show $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ are mutually inverse bijections, and verify the stated properties.

---

## Preliminary lemmas

<Theorem title="Lemma 1: Artin's theorem" number="P.1">
Let $G$ be a finite group of automorphisms of a field $E$. Then $[E : E^G] = |G|$ and $E/E^G$ is Galois with $\operatorname{Gal}(E/E^G) = G$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
Let $F = E^G$ and $n = |G|$. We show $[E:F] \le n$ and $[E:F] \ge n$.

**$[E:F] \le n$**: Suppose $[E:F] > n$. Then there exist $n+1$ elements $v_1, \ldots, v_{n+1} \in E$ that are linearly independent over $F$. Let $G = \{\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n\}$. Consider the system of $n$ linear equations in $n+1$ unknowns:

$$\sum_{j=1}^{n+1} \sigma_i(v_j) x_j = 0, \quad i = 1, \ldots, n.$$

This system has more unknowns than equations, so it has a nontrivial solution $(x_1, \ldots, x_{n+1}) \in E^{n+1}$, not all zero. Choose a solution with the minimal number of nonzero entries. WLOG $x_1 \neq 0$; by dividing, assume $x_1 = 1$.

If all $x_j \in F$, then taking $i$ for $\sigma_i = \operatorname{id}$: $\sum_j v_j x_j = 0$, contradicting linear independence of $v_j$ over $F$. So some $x_j \notin F$, say $x_2 \notin F$. Then there exists $\sigma_k \in G$ with $\sigma_k(x_2) \neq x_2$.

Apply $\sigma_k$ to the system: $\sum_j \sigma_k\sigma_i(v_j) \sigma_k(x_j) = 0$. Since $\sigma_k$ permutes the elements of $G$, we get $\sum_j \sigma_m(v_j) \sigma_k(x_j) = 0$ for all $m$. Subtracting from the original: $\sum_j \sigma_m(v_j)(x_j - \sigma_k(x_j)) = 0$ for all $m$.

This gives a solution with $x_1 - \sigma_k(x_1) = 1 - 1 = 0$ (a zero entry where before there was a nonzero entry) and $x_2 - \sigma_k(x_2) \neq 0$ (so the solution is nontrivial). This contradicts minimality of the number of nonzero entries.

**$[E:F] \ge n$**: Since $G \le \operatorname{Aut}(E/F)$ and any automorphism of $E$ fixing $F$ is determined by its action on a basis, distinct automorphisms give distinct permutations. We use a different counting: any $\alpha \in E$ has at most $n$ images $\{\sigma(\alpha) \mid \sigma \in G\}$, so the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ over $F$ has degree at most $n$. But we also need $|\operatorname{Aut}(E/F)| \ge |G| = n$.

Actually, by the linear independence of characters (Dedekind's lemma), the $n$ distinct homomorphisms $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n: E^\times \to E^\times$ are linearly independent over $E$. This forces $[E:F] \ge n$.

**$\operatorname{Gal}(E/F) = G$**: We have $G \le \operatorname{Gal}(E/F)$ and $|\operatorname{Gal}(E/F)| \le [E:F] = |G|$, so $\operatorname{Gal}(E/F) = G$.
</Proof>

<Theorem title="Lemma 2: Dedekind's independence of characters" number="P.2">
Let $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n: E \to L$ be distinct field homomorphisms from a field $E$ to a field $L$. Then $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n$ are linearly independent over $L$: if $\sum_{i=1}^n c_i \sigma_i(x) = 0$ for all $x \in E$ with $c_i \in L$, then all $c_i = 0$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
By induction on $n$. The case $n = 1$ is clear ($c_1 \sigma_1 \neq 0$ since $\sigma_1 \neq 0$).

Suppose $n > 1$ and $\sum_{i=1}^n c_i \sigma_i = 0$ is a nontrivial relation with the fewest nonzero terms. All $c_i$ must be nonzero (otherwise we have a shorter relation). Since $\sigma_1 \neq \sigma_n$, there exists $a \in E$ with $\sigma_1(a) \neq \sigma_n(a)$.

Substituting $ax$ for $x$: $\sum_i c_i \sigma_i(a) \sigma_i(x) = 0$ for all $x$.

Multiplying the original relation by $\sigma_n(a)$: $\sum_i c_i \sigma_n(a) \sigma_i(x) = 0$ for all $x$.

Subtracting: $\sum_i c_i(\sigma_i(a) - \sigma_n(a))\sigma_i(x) = 0$ for all $x$.

The $n$-th coefficient vanishes (since $\sigma_n(a) - \sigma_n(a) = 0$), and the first coefficient $c_1(\sigma_1(a) - \sigma_n(a)) \neq 0$. This gives a shorter nontrivial relation, contradicting minimality.
</Proof>

## Main proof

<Theorem title="Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory" number="P.3">
The maps $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ are mutually inverse inclusion-reversing bijections.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
**Part A: $\Psi \circ \Phi = \operatorname{id}$ (i.e., $E^{\operatorname{Gal}(E/K)} = K$ for every intermediate field $K$).**

Let $K$ be an intermediate field. Since $E/F$ is Galois (separable and normal), $E/K$ is also separable and normal (separability is inherited by subextensions, and normality is inherited since $E$ is a splitting field over $F$, hence also over $K$). So $E/K$ is Galois.

By Artin's theorem applied to $H = \operatorname{Gal}(E/K)$: $E^H = K$ and $|H| = [E:K]$. In particular, $\Psi(\Phi(K)) = E^{\operatorname{Gal}(E/K)} = K$.

**Part B: $\Phi \circ \Psi = \operatorname{id}$ (i.e., $\operatorname{Gal}(E/E^H) = H$ for every subgroup $H \le G$).**

Let $H \le G$. By Artin's theorem, $[E : E^H] = |H|$ and $\operatorname{Gal}(E/E^H) = H$.

**Part C: Inclusion reversal.**

If $K_1 \subseteq K_2$, then any $\sigma$ fixing $K_2$ also fixes $K_1$, so $\operatorname{Gal}(E/K_2) \le \operatorname{Gal}(E/K_1)$, i.e., $\Phi(K_1) \ge \Phi(K_2)$. Similarly, if $H_1 \le H_2$, then $E^{H_2} \subseteq E^{H_1}$, i.e., $\Psi(H_1) \ge \Psi(H_2)$.

**Part D: Degree-index relation.**

$[E:K] = |\operatorname{Gal}(E/K)|$ (since $E/K$ is Galois). By the tower law, $[K:F] = [E:F]/[E:K] = |G|/|\operatorname{Gal}(E/K)| = [G:\operatorname{Gal}(E/K)]$.
</Proof>

## Proof of the normality criterion

<Theorem title="Normality criterion" number="P.4">
An intermediate field $K$ gives a normal (hence Galois) extension $K/F$ if and only if $H = \operatorname{Gal}(E/K) \trianglelefteq G$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
**$(\Rightarrow)$** Suppose $K/F$ is normal. For any $\sigma \in G$ and $\alpha \in K$, the minimal polynomial $p(x)$ of $\alpha$ over $F$ splits in $K$ (by normality). Since $\sigma(\alpha)$ is also a root of $p$, we have $\sigma(\alpha) \in K$. So $\sigma(K) = K$ for all $\sigma \in G$.

Now for any $\sigma \in G$ and $\tau \in H$: $\sigma\tau\sigma^{-1}$ fixes $\sigma(K) = K$ pointwise? No, we need more care. For $\alpha \in K$: $(\sigma\tau\sigma^{-1})(\alpha) = \sigma(\tau(\sigma^{-1}(\alpha)))$. Since $\sigma^{-1}(\alpha) \in \sigma^{-1}(K) = K$ (by $\sigma(K) = K$), and $\tau$ fixes $K$, we get $\tau(\sigma^{-1}(\alpha)) = \sigma^{-1}(\alpha)$, so $(\sigma\tau\sigma^{-1})(\alpha) = \sigma(\sigma^{-1}(\alpha)) = \alpha$. Thus $\sigma\tau\sigma^{-1} \in H$, proving $H \trianglelefteq G$.

**$(\Leftarrow)$** Suppose $H \trianglelefteq G$. We must show $K/F$ is normal. Let $\alpha \in K$ and $p(x) = \min_F(\alpha)$. Every root of $p$ in $E$ has the form $\sigma(\alpha)$ for some $\sigma \in G$.

**Claim**: $\sigma(\alpha) \in K$ for all $\sigma \in G$. For any $\tau \in H$: $\tau(\sigma(\alpha)) = \sigma(\sigma^{-1}\tau\sigma)(\alpha)$. Since $H$ is normal, $\sigma^{-1}\tau\sigma \in H$, so $(\sigma^{-1}\tau\sigma)(\alpha) = \alpha$ (as $\alpha \in K = E^H$). Thus $\tau(\sigma(\alpha)) = \sigma(\alpha)$, meaning $\sigma(\alpha) \in E^H = K$.

So all roots of $p$ in $E$ lie in $K$, and $p$ splits in $K$. Since $p$ was the minimal polynomial of an arbitrary $\alpha \in K$, the extension $K/F$ is normal.
</Proof>

## Proof that Gal(K/F) is the quotient

<Theorem title="Quotient isomorphism" number="P.5">
When $K/F$ is normal (equivalently $H \trianglelefteq G$), there is a natural isomorphism $\operatorname{Gal}(K/F) \cong G/H$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
Define the restriction map $\pi: G \to \operatorname{Gal}(K/F)$ by $\pi(\sigma) = \sigma|_K$. This is well-defined because $\sigma(K) = K$ (proved above when $K/F$ is normal).

$\pi$ is a group homomorphism: $\pi(\sigma\tau) = (\sigma\tau)|_K = \sigma|_K \circ \tau|_K = \pi(\sigma)\pi(\tau)$.

$\ker \pi = \{\sigma \in G \mid \sigma|_K = \operatorname{id}_K\} = \operatorname{Gal}(E/K) = H$.

$\pi$ is surjective: $|\operatorname{im}(\pi)| = |G|/|H| = [G:H] = [K:F] = |\operatorname{Gal}(K/F)|$ (the last equality because $K/F$ is Galois).

By the first isomorphism theorem, $G/H \cong \operatorname{Gal}(K/F)$.
</Proof>

## Verification with examples

<Example id="verify-v4" title="Verification for Q(sqrt(2), sqrt(3))/Q">
$G = V_4 = \{e, \sigma_1, \sigma_2, \sigma_3\}$ where $\sigma_1$ negates $\sqrt{2}$, $\sigma_2$ negates $\sqrt{3}$, $\sigma_3$ negates both.

| Subgroup $H$ | $E^H$ | $[E^H:\mathbb{Q}]$ | $[G:H]$ | $H \trianglelefteq G$? | $E^H/\mathbb{Q}$ normal? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| $\{e\}$ | $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3})$ | $4$ | $4$ | Yes | Yes |
| $\langle\sigma_1\rangle$ | $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{3})$ | $2$ | $2$ | Yes | Yes |
| $\langle\sigma_2\rangle$ | $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ | $2$ | $2$ | Yes | Yes |
| $\langle\sigma_3\rangle$ | $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{6})$ | $2$ | $2$ | Yes | Yes |
| $V_4$ | $\mathbb{Q}$ | $1$ | $1$ | Yes | Yes |

All subgroups are normal (since $V_4$ is abelian), and all intermediate fields are Galois over $\mathbb{Q}$.
</Example>

<Example id="verify-s3" title="Verification for Q(cbrt(2), omega)/Q">
$G \cong S_3$. Normal subgroups: $\{e\}$, $A_3 = \langle(123)\rangle$, $S_3$.

The subgroups of order $2$ ($\langle(12)\rangle$, $\langle(13)\rangle$, $\langle(23)\rangle$) are **not** normal.

Correspondingly:
- $E^{A_3} = \mathbb{Q}(\omega)$ and $\mathbb{Q}(\omega)/\mathbb{Q}$ is normal ($\Leftrightarrow A_3 \trianglelefteq S_3$), with $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\omega)/\mathbb{Q}) \cong S_3/A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.
- $E^{\langle(23)\rangle} = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$ and $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})/\mathbb{Q}$ is **not** normal ($\Leftrightarrow \langle(23)\rangle \not\trianglelefteq S_3$).
</Example>

<Example id="verify-d4" title="Verification for the D_4 example">
For $E = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2}, i)$ with $G \cong D_4$:

$D_4$ has three normal subgroups of order $4$: $\langle r \rangle$, $\langle r^2, s \rangle$, $\langle r^2, rs \rangle$. The corresponding fixed fields are $\mathbb{Q}(i)$, $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$, $\mathbb{Q}(i\sqrt{2})$, all of degree $2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, and all are normal extensions.

The center $Z(D_4) = \langle r^2 \rangle$ has index $4$. The fixed field $E^{\langle r^2 \rangle} = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, i)$ has degree $4$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, and $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2},i)/\mathbb{Q}) \cong D_4/\langle r^2 \rangle \cong V_4$.
</Example>

<Example id="verify-cyclic" title="Verification for a cyclic extension">
$\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_7)/\mathbb{Q}$ with $G \cong \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$. Every subgroup of a cyclic group is normal, so every intermediate field is Galois over $\mathbb{Q}$.

Subgroups and fixed fields:
- $\{0\} \leftrightarrow \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_7)$ (degree $6$)
- $2\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \leftrightarrow$ the unique cubic subfield (degree $3$)
- $3\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \leftrightarrow \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-7})$ (degree $2$)
- $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \leftrightarrow \mathbb{Q}$ (degree $1$)
</Example>

<Example id="verify-compositum" title="Compositum via the theorem">
If $H_1, H_2 \le G$, then:
- $E^{H_1} \cap E^{H_2} = E^{\langle H_1, H_2 \rangle}$ (intersection of fields corresponds to join of subgroups)
- $E^{H_1} \cdot E^{H_2} = E^{H_1 \cap H_2}$ (compositum of fields corresponds to intersection of subgroups)

For the $V_4$ example: $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) \cap \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{3}) = E^{\langle\sigma_1,\sigma_2\rangle} = E^{V_4} = \mathbb{Q}$, confirming that the two quadratic fields intersect only in $\mathbb{Q}$.
</Example>

## Summary of the proof structure

The proof of the Fundamental Theorem rests on three pillars:

1. **Artin's theorem** provides the key identity $[E : E^H] = |H|$, linking degrees and group orders.

2. **Dedekind's lemma** (independence of characters) is the technical heart, ensuring that distinct automorphisms cannot "collapse" in a vector space sense.

3. **The normality criterion** translates normal subgroups to normal extensions via the restriction homomorphism and the first isomorphism theorem.

<Remark title="Infinite Galois theory">
For infinite Galois extensions, the bijection must be modified: one considers **closed** subgroups of the Galois group (in the Krull topology, also called the profinite topology). The correspondence becomes: intermediate fields $\leftrightarrow$ closed subgroups. Every subgroup of finite index is closed, but infinite-index subgroups need not be. This is the content of the **Krull topology** version of the Fundamental Theorem.
</Remark>
