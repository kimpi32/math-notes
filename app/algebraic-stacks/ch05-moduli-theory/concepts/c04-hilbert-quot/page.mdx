# Hilbert and Quot Schemes

The Hilbert scheme and the Quot scheme are among the most important representable moduli problems in algebraic geometry. They parametrize subschemes and quotient sheaves, respectively, and their representability (proved by Grothendieck) is a cornerstone of modern moduli theory.

---

## 1. The Hilbert Functor

<Definition title="Hilbert Polynomial">
Let $X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^n_k$ be a projective scheme over a field $k$, and let $\mathcal{O}_X(1)$ be the restriction of the twisting sheaf. The **Hilbert polynomial** of $X$ is the numerical polynomial $P_X \in \mathbb{Q}[t]$ such that
$$P_X(m) = \chi(\mathcal{O}_X(m)) = \sum_{i=0}^{\dim X} (-1)^i h^i(X, \mathcal{O}_X(m))$$
for all $m \gg 0$. By Serre's vanishing theorem, $P_X(m) = h^0(X, \mathcal{O}_X(m))$ for $m \gg 0$.
</Definition>

<Definition title="Hilbert Functor">
Let $X \to S$ be a projective morphism and $\mathcal{O}_X(1)$ a relatively ample line bundle. For a numerical polynomial $P$, the **Hilbert functor** is
$$\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S} : (\mathsf{Sch}/S)^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Sets}$$
$$T \mapsto \left\lbrace Z \hookrightarrow X_T \;\middle|\; Z \text{ is a closed subscheme, flat over } T, \text{ with Hilbert polynomial } P \right\rbrace$$
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Grothendieck's Representability Theorem">
Let $X \to S$ be a projective morphism with $S$ Noetherian. Then the Hilbert functor $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$ is representable by a projective $S$-scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$.
</Theorem>

## 2. Examples of Hilbert Schemes

<Example title="Hilbert Scheme of Points: $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$">
When $P(t) = n$ is a constant polynomial, $\mathrm{Hilb}^n_{X/k}$ parametrizes 0-dimensional subschemes of length $n$. This is the **Hilbert scheme of $n$ points**.

For $X = \mathbb{A}^2$ (or any smooth surface), $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ is a smooth variety of dimension $2n$ (Fogarty's theorem). It comes with a Hilbert-Chow morphism
$$\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X) \to \mathrm{Sym}^n(X) = X^n / \mathfrak{S}_n$$
that is a resolution of singularities.
</Example>

<Example title="$\mathrm{Hilb}^2(\mathbb{A}^2)$">
The Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^2(\mathbb{A}^2)$ parametrizes ideals $I \subset k[x,y]$ with $\dim_k k[x,y]/I = 2$. These are either:
- Two distinct points: $I = (x - a_1, y - b_1) \cap (x - a_2, y - b_2)$, or
- A "fat point" with a tangent direction: $I = (x^2, y - \lambda x)$ at the origin (up to translation).

The scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^2(\mathbb{A}^2) \cong \mathrm{Bl}_{\Delta}(\mathbb{A}^2 \times \mathbb{A}^2) / \mathfrak{S}_2$, the blowup of the symmetric product along the diagonal. It is smooth of dimension 4.
</Example>

<Example title="$\mathrm{Hilb}^n(\mathbb{A}^1)$">
For the affine line, $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(\mathbb{A}^1) \cong \mathbb{A}^n$. An ideal $I \subset k[x]$ of colength $n$ is generated by a unique monic polynomial of degree $n$: $I = (x^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0)$. The coefficients give the isomorphism with $\mathbb{A}^n$.
</Example>

<Example title="Hilbert Scheme of Curves in $\mathbb{P}^3$">
The Hilbert polynomial of a curve of degree $d$ and arithmetic genus $g$ in $\mathbb{P}^3$ is $P(t) = dt + 1 - g$. The Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^{dt + 1 - g}_{\mathbb{P}^3}$ has components:
- **Twisted cubics** ($d = 3, g = 0$): $P(t) = 3t + 1$. The component parametrizing smooth twisted cubics has dimension 12.
- **Lines** ($d = 1, g = 0$): $P(t) = t + 1$. This component is the Grassmannian $\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4) = \mathrm{Gr}(1, \mathbb{P}^3)$, dimension 4.
- **Plane conics** ($d = 2, g = 0$): $P(t) = 2t + 1$. Dimension 8 (choice of plane + conic in the plane).
</Example>

<Example title="Hilbert Scheme of Hypersurfaces">
A hypersurface of degree $d$ in $\mathbb{P}^n$ is a closed subscheme defined by a single homogeneous polynomial. The Hilbert polynomial is $P(t) = \binom{t+n}{n} - \binom{t+n-d}{n}$. The Hilbert scheme is
$$\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{\mathbb{P}^n} \cong \mathbb{P}^{\binom{n+d}{d} - 1}$$
the projectivization of the space of degree-$d$ forms. For $n = 2, d = 3$: plane cubics form $\mathbb{P}^9$.
</Example>

## 3. The Quot Functor

<Definition title="Quot Functor">
Let $X \to S$ be a projective morphism, $\mathcal{E}$ a coherent sheaf on $X$, and $P$ a numerical polynomial. The **Quot functor** is
$$\mathrm{Quot}^P_{\mathcal{E}/X/S} : (\mathsf{Sch}/S)^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathsf{Sets}$$
$$T \mapsto \left\lbrace \mathcal{E}_T \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{F} \;\middle|\; \mathcal{F} \text{ is flat over } T \text{ with Hilbert polynomial } P \right\rbrace / \cong$$
where $\mathcal{E}_T$ is the pullback of $\mathcal{E}$ to $X_T$, and two quotients are equivalent if they have the same kernel.
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Representability of Quot">
The Quot functor $\mathrm{Quot}^P_{\mathcal{E}/X/S}$ is representable by a projective $S$-scheme.
</Theorem>

Note that the Hilbert scheme is a special case: $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S} = \mathrm{Quot}^P_{\mathcal{O}_X/X/S}$.

<Example title="Grassmannian as Quot Scheme">
The Grassmannian is the Quot scheme:
$$\mathrm{Gr}(k, n) = \mathrm{Quot}^k_{\mathcal{O}^n_{\mathrm{pt}} / \mathrm{pt} / \mathrm{pt}}$$
parametrizing rank-$k$ quotients of $\mathcal{O}^n$. More generally, the flag variety $\mathrm{Fl}(d_1, \ldots, d_r; n)$ can be realized as an iterated Quot scheme.
</Example>

<Example title="Quot Scheme of Points on a Curve">
For a smooth curve $C$ and $\mathcal{E} = \mathcal{O}_C^r$, the Quot scheme $\mathrm{Quot}^n_{\mathcal{O}_C^r / C}$ (quotients of length $n$) has dimension $rn$. For $r = 1$, this is $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(C) = C^{(n)} = \mathrm{Sym}^n(C)$, which is smooth of dimension $n$.
</Example>

## 4. Tangent Space and Obstruction Theory

<Theorem title="Tangent Space to Hilbert Scheme">
Let $[Z] \in \mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$ be a point corresponding to a closed subscheme $Z \hookrightarrow X$ with ideal sheaf $\mathcal{I}_Z$. The **Zariski tangent space** at $[Z]$ is
$$T_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S} \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_Z}(\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2, \mathcal{O}_Z) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_X}(\mathcal{I}_Z, \mathcal{O}_Z)$$
The **obstruction space** is contained in $\mathrm{Ext}^1_{\mathcal{O}_X}(\mathcal{I}_Z, \mathcal{O}_Z)$.
</Theorem>

<Example title="Tangent Space for Points in $\mathbb{A}^2$">
For $Z = \lbrace p \rbrace \subset \mathbb{A}^2$ a single reduced point, $\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2 \cong \mathfrak{m}_p / \mathfrak{m}_p^2$, the cotangent space. So
$$T_{[p]} \mathrm{Hilb}^1(\mathbb{A}^2) = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathfrak{m}_p/\mathfrak{m}_p^2, k) = T_p \mathbb{A}^2 \cong k^2,$$
consistent with $\mathrm{Hilb}^1(\mathbb{A}^2) \cong \mathbb{A}^2$.
</Example>

<Example title="Tangent Space for Hypersurfaces">
For a smooth hypersurface $Z = V(f) \subset \mathbb{P}^n$ of degree $d$, the ideal sheaf is $\mathcal{I}_Z = \mathcal{O}(-d)$. Then
$$T_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb} = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathcal{O}(-d), \mathcal{O}_Z) = H^0(Z, \mathcal{O}_Z(d)) = H^0(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(d)) / \langle f \rangle$$
The dimension is $\binom{n+d}{d} - 1$, confirming that hypersurfaces of degree $d$ form $\mathbb{P}^{\binom{n+d}{d}-1}$.
</Example>

<Theorem title="Tangent Space to Quot Scheme">
For a point $[\mathcal{E} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{F}]$ of $\mathrm{Quot}^P_{\mathcal{E}/X/S}$ with kernel $\mathcal{K} = \ker(\mathcal{E} \to \mathcal{F})$:
$$T \, \mathrm{Quot}^P_{\mathcal{E}/X/S} \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_X}(\mathcal{K}, \mathcal{F})$$
Obstructions lie in $\mathrm{Ext}^1_{\mathcal{O}_X}(\mathcal{K}, \mathcal{F})$.
</Theorem>

## 5. Smoothness and Dimension

<Example title="Fogarty's Theorem">
For a smooth projective surface $S$, $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(S)$ is smooth of dimension $2n$. The proof uses the fact that for a length-$n$ subscheme $Z \subset S$:
$$\mathrm{Ext}^1(\mathcal{I}_Z, \mathcal{O}_Z) \cong \mathrm{Ext}^2(\mathcal{O}_Z, \mathcal{O}_Z) = 0$$
by Serre duality on the surface (since $\mathrm{Hom}(\mathcal{O}_Z, \mathcal{O}_Z \otimes \omega_S) \cong H^0(Z, \omega_S|_Z)$, which relates to the obstruction space, and the vanishing follows from dimension reasons on $Z$).
</Example>

<Example title="Hilbert Scheme of Lines on a Cubic Threefold">
Let $X \subset \mathbb{P}^4$ be a smooth cubic threefold. The Hilbert scheme of lines (subschemes with Hilbert polynomial $P(t) = t + 1$) on $X$ is the **Fano surface** $F(X)$. It is a smooth surface of general type with:
- $\dim F(X) = 2$
- $\chi(\mathcal{O}_{F(X)}) = 1$
- The Albanese variety of $F(X)$ is the intermediate Jacobian $J(X)$, a principally polarized abelian variety of dimension 5.
</Example>

<Example title="Hilbert Scheme of Lines on a Cubic Fourfold">
For a smooth cubic fourfold $X \subset \mathbb{P}^5$, the Fano variety of lines $F(X)$ is a smooth projective 4-fold. By Beauville-Donagi, $F(X)$ is a hyperkahler manifold deformation-equivalent to $\mathrm{Hilb}^2(K3)$. It carries a natural symplectic form.
</Example>

## 6. Hilbert Schemes and Moduli of Sheaves

<Definition title="Moduli of Sheaves via Quot">
The moduli of semistable sheaves on a projective variety $X$ with fixed Hilbert polynomial $P$ can be constructed using the Quot scheme. Choose $m \gg 0$ so that every semistable sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ with polynomial $P$ is $m$-regular. Then $\mathcal{F}$ is a quotient of $\mathcal{O}_X(-m)^{P(m)}$, giving a point of $\mathrm{Quot}^P_{\mathcal{O}_X(-m)^{P(m)}/X}$. The moduli space is constructed as a GIT quotient of the appropriate open subset of this Quot scheme by $\mathrm{GL}_{P(m)}$.
</Definition>

<Example title="Moduli of Sheaves on $\mathbb{P}^2$">
Rank-2 torsion-free sheaves on $\mathbb{P}^2$ with $c_1 = 0$ and $c_2 = n$ have moduli space $M(2, 0, n)$. By the ADHM construction, $M(2, 0, n)$ can be described as a GIT quotient:
$$M(2, 0, n) \cong \left\lbrace (B_1, B_2, I, J) \;\middle|\; [B_1, B_2] + IJ = 0, \text{ stability} \right\rbrace / \mathrm{GL}_n$$
where $B_1, B_2 \in \mathrm{End}(V)$, $I \in \mathrm{Hom}(W, V)$, $J \in \mathrm{Hom}(V, W)$ with $\dim V = n$, $\dim W = 2$.
</Example>

## 7. Relative Hilbert Schemes and Applications

<Example title="Relative Hilbert Scheme of a Family of Curves">
Let $f: \mathcal{C} \to B$ be a family of smooth curves of genus $g$. The relative Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^n_{\mathcal{C}/B}$ parametrizes effective divisors of degree $n$ on the fibers. Its fiber over $b \in B$ is $\mathrm{Sym}^n(C_b)$. When $n \geq 2g - 1$, the Abel-Jacobi map $\mathrm{Hilb}^n_{\mathcal{C}/B} \to \mathrm{Pic}^n_{\mathcal{C}/B}$ is a $\mathbb{P}^{n-g}$-bundle.
</Example>

<Example title="Nested Hilbert Schemes">
The **nested Hilbert scheme** $\mathrm{Hilb}^{n, n+1}(S)$ for a surface $S$ parametrizes pairs $(Z, Z')$ where $Z \subset Z'$ are subschemes of lengths $n$ and $n+1$. It fits into a diagram:
$$\mathrm{Hilb}^{n,n+1}(S) \xrightarrow{p_1} \mathrm{Hilb}^n(S)$$
$$\phantom{\mathrm{Hilb}^{n,n+1}(S)} \xrightarrow{p_2} \mathrm{Hilb}^{n+1}(S)$$
The fiber of $p_1$ over $[Z]$ is the blowup $\mathrm{Bl}_Z(S)$. These nested structures play a role in the theory of Heisenberg algebras acting on $\bigoplus_n H^*(\mathrm{Hilb}^n(S))$ (Nakajima).
</Example>
