# Quadratic Form

A quadratic form is a homogeneous polynomial of degree $2$ in the coordinates of a vector. Quadratic forms are intimately connected to symmetric bilinear forms: each determines the other (in characteristic $\neq 2$). They arise throughout mathematics -- in optimization, number theory, differential geometry, and physics.

---

## Definition

<Definition title="Quadratic form" number="8.7">
A **quadratic form** on a vector space $V$ over $F$ is a function $Q: V \to F$ such that:

1. $Q(\alpha v) = \alpha^2 Q(v)$ for all $\alpha \in F$, $v \in V$.
2. The function $B_Q(u, v) = Q(u + v) - Q(u) - Q(v)$ is bilinear.

In characteristic $\neq 2$, $Q$ and its associated symmetric bilinear form $B(u, v) = \frac{1}{2}B_Q(u,v) = \frac{1}{2}(Q(u+v) - Q(u) - Q(v))$ determine each other via:

$$Q(v) = B(v, v), \quad B(u, v) = \frac{1}{2}(Q(u + v) - Q(u) - Q(v)).$$
</Definition>

<Example id="qf-R2" title="Quadratic forms on R^2">
$Q(x, y) = ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$ (the most general quadratic form on $\mathbb{R}^2$).

Associated bilinear form: $B((x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2)) = ax_1 x_2 + b(x_1 y_2 + x_2 y_1) + cy_1 y_2$.

Gram matrix: $M = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{pmatrix}$.

- $Q = x^2 + y^2$: $M = I$ (positive definite).
- $Q = x^2 - y^2$: $M = \operatorname{diag}(1, -1)$ (indefinite).
- $Q = 2xy$: $M = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ (indefinite, $\det = -1$).
- $Q = x^2$: $M = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ (degenerate, rank $1$).
</Example>

<Example id="qf-R3" title="Quadratic forms on R^3">
$Q(x, y, z) = x^2 + 2y^2 + 3z^2 + 2xy - 4xz$. The matrix is:

$M = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 2 & 0 \\ -2 & 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$.

The coefficient of $x_i x_j$ (with $i \neq j$) is $2m_{ij}$, so $m_{12} = 1$ (half of the coefficient $2$ of $xy$) and $m_{13} = -2$ (half of $-4$).
</Example>

## Classification: positive definite, negative definite, indefinite

<Definition title="Definiteness of a quadratic form" number="8.8">
A real quadratic form $Q$ (or its matrix $M$) is:
- **Positive definite** if $Q(v) > 0$ for all $v \neq 0$.
- **Positive semi-definite** if $Q(v) \geq 0$ for all $v$.
- **Negative definite** if $Q(v) < 0$ for all $v \neq 0$.
- **Negative semi-definite** if $Q(v) \leq 0$ for all $v$.
- **Indefinite** if $Q$ takes both positive and negative values.
</Definition>

<Example id="definiteness-2x2" title="Classifying 2x2 quadratic forms">
$Q = ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$ with $M = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{pmatrix}$.

- **Positive definite** iff $a > 0$ and $\det M = ac - b^2 > 0$.
- **Negative definite** iff $a < 0$ and $ac - b^2 > 0$.
- **Indefinite** iff $ac - b^2 < 0$.
- **Semi-definite** (degenerate) iff $ac - b^2 = 0$.

Examples:
- $Q = x^2 + y^2$: $a = 1, b = 0, c = 1$. $\det = 1 > 0$, $a > 0$. Positive definite.
- $Q = x^2 - y^2$: $\det = -1 < 0$. Indefinite.
- $Q = x^2 + 2xy + y^2 = (x + y)^2$: $\det = 0$. Positive semi-definite (but not positive definite).
</Example>

<Example id="definiteness-3x3" title="Sylvester's criterion for 3x3">
$M = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$.

Leading principal minors: $\Delta_1 = 2 > 0$, $\Delta_2 = 6 - 1 = 5 > 0$, $\Delta_3 = \det M = 2(12 - 1) - 1(4) = 18 > 0$.

All positive, so $M$ is **positive definite**.
</Example>

## Completing the square

<Theorem title="Diagonalization by completing the square">
Every real quadratic form can be reduced to a sum of squares (with signs) by a change of variables:

$$Q(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = d_1 y_1^2 + d_2 y_2^2 + \cdots + d_n y_n^2$$

where $y_i$ are linear combinations of $x_j$ and $d_i \in \{+1, -1, 0\}$ (after further scaling).
</Theorem>

<Example id="complete-square-2" title="Completing the square in 2 variables">
$Q(x, y) = 2x^2 + 4xy + 5y^2$.

$= 2(x^2 + 2xy) + 5y^2 = 2(x + y)^2 - 2y^2 + 5y^2 = 2(x + y)^2 + 3y^2$.

Set $u = x + y$, $v = y$: $Q = 2u^2 + 3v^2$ (sum of two positive squares, so positive definite).

Matrix check: $M = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$, $\det = 6 > 0$, $a = 2 > 0$. Positive definite ✓.
</Example>

<Example id="complete-square-3" title="Completing the square in 3 variables">
$Q(x, y, z) = x^2 + 2xy + 2y^2 + 2yz + z^2$.

$= (x + y)^2 + y^2 + 2yz + z^2 = (x + y)^2 + (y + z)^2$.

Set $u = x + y$, $v = y + z$, $w = z$: $Q = u^2 + v^2$.

The form has rank $2$ (only $2$ squares). Signature $(2, 0)$ with rank $2$ out of $3$ variables, so it is positive semi-definite but degenerate.
</Example>

<Example id="complete-square-indefinite" title="Completing the square: indefinite case">
$Q(x, y) = x^2 - 4xy + y^2$.

$= (x - 2y)^2 - 4y^2 + y^2 = (x - 2y)^2 - 3y^2$.

Set $u = x - 2y$, $v = y$: $Q = u^2 - 3v^2$ (indefinite, signature $(1, 1)$).
</Example>

## The signature

<Definition title="Signature of a quadratic form" number="8.9">
After diagonalization, $Q = d_1 y_1^2 + \cdots + d_r y_r^2$ where $d_i \neq 0$ and $r = \operatorname{rank}(Q)$. Let $p$ be the number of positive $d_i$ and $q$ the number of negative $d_i$. The **signature** of $Q$ is the pair $(p, q)$, with $p + q = r$.

By Sylvester's Law of Inertia, the signature is independent of the choice of diagonalization.
</Definition>

<Example id="signatures" title="Signatures of common forms">
- $Q = x^2 + y^2 + z^2$: signature $(3, 0)$ (positive definite).
- $Q = x^2 + y^2 - z^2$: signature $(2, 1)$ (indefinite, the "light cone" form).
- $Q = -x^2 - y^2 - z^2$: signature $(0, 3)$ (negative definite).
- $Q = x^2 - y^2$: signature $(1, 1)$ (hyperbolic form).
- $Q = x^2 + y^2$: on $\mathbb{R}^3$, this has signature $(2, 0)$ and rank $2$ (degenerate).
</Example>

## Quadratic forms in applications

<Example id="qf-optimization" title="Quadratic forms in optimization">
The second-order Taylor expansion of $f(x)$ at a critical point $x_0$ is:

$$f(x_0 + h) \approx f(x_0) + \frac{1}{2} h^T H h,$$

where $H$ is the Hessian matrix. The nature of the critical point depends on the quadratic form $Q(h) = h^T H h$:
- $Q$ positive definite: local minimum.
- $Q$ negative definite: local maximum.
- $Q$ indefinite: saddle point.

For $f(x, y) = x^2 + xy + y^2$ at $(0, 0)$: $H = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, eigenvalues $3, 1 > 0$. Local minimum ✓.
</Example>

<Example id="qf-conic" title="Classifying conics">
The conic $ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2 + dx + ey + f = 0$ is classified by the quadratic part $Q = ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$:

- $ac - b^2 > 0$: ellipse (or circle if $a = c$ and $b = 0$).
- $ac - b^2 < 0$: hyperbola.
- $ac - b^2 = 0$: parabola.

$x^2 + y^2 = 1$ (circle): $a = c = 1, b = 0$, $ac - b^2 = 1 > 0$.
$x^2 - y^2 = 1$ (hyperbola): $ac - b^2 = -1 < 0$.
$x^2 = y$ (parabola): $a = 1, b = c = 0$, $ac - b^2 = 0$.
</Example>

<Example id="qf-number-theory" title="Quadratic forms in number theory">
The question "which integers are sums of two squares?" is equivalent to asking for which $n$ the equation $Q(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 = n$ has integer solutions.

Fermat's theorem: a prime $p$ is a sum of two squares iff $p = 2$ or $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$.

The form $Q(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$ has discriminant $-4$. Forms of different discriminants represent different sets of integers. This is the beginning of the theory of binary quadratic forms (Gauss).
</Example>

<Example id="qf-moment-inertia" title="Moment of inertia tensor">
The moment of inertia of a rigid body about an axis $\hat{n}$ is $I = \hat{n}^T \mathcal{I} \hat{n}$, a quadratic form in the direction $\hat{n}$. The inertia tensor $\mathcal{I}$ is a $3 \times 3$ positive semi-definite symmetric matrix.

The principal axes are the eigenvectors of $\mathcal{I}$, and the principal moments of inertia are the eigenvalues.

For a uniform cube of side $a$ and mass $m$: $\mathcal{I} = \frac{ma^2}{6}I_3$ (isotropic), so $I = \frac{ma^2}{6}$ regardless of the axis direction.
</Example>

## Equivalence of quadratic forms

<Definition title="Equivalence of quadratic forms" number="8.10">
Two quadratic forms $Q$ and $Q'$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ are **equivalent** (over $\mathbb{R}$) if there is an invertible linear change of variables taking one to the other, equivalently if their Gram matrices are congruent: $M' = P^T M P$ for some invertible $P$.

By Sylvester's Law, real quadratic forms are equivalent iff they have the same rank and signature.
</Definition>

<Example id="equivalence" title="Equivalent quadratic forms">
$Q_1 = x^2 + y^2$ and $Q_2 = 2x^2 + 3y^2$: both have signature $(2, 0)$ and rank $2$. They are equivalent over $\mathbb{R}$ (set $u = \sqrt{2}x$, $v = \sqrt{3}y$: $Q_2 = u^2 + v^2 = Q_1$).

$Q_1 = x^2 - y^2$ and $Q_2 = 2xy$: both have signature $(1, 1)$. They are equivalent (set $u = x + y$, $v = x - y$: $2xy = u^2/2 - v^2/2$... after scaling, same signature).

$Q_1 = x^2 + y^2$ and $Q_2 = x^2 - y^2$: signatures $(2, 0)$ and $(1, 1)$. Not equivalent.
</Example>

## Summary

<Remark title="Quadratic forms as geometry">
Quadratic forms bridge algebra and geometry:
- Their **definiteness** classifies critical points in optimization.
- Their **signature** classifies real quadratic forms up to equivalence (Sylvester).
- In number theory, they determine which integers can be represented.
- In physics, they appear as energy forms, inertia tensors, and metrics.
- The passage from $Q$ to its associated bilinear form $B$ (and back) is the bridge between "self-pairing" and "cross-pairing" data.
</Remark>
