# Proof of the Rank-Nullity Theorem

We prove: if $T : V \to W$ is linear and $V$ is finite-dimensional, then $\dim V = \text{rank}(T) + \text{nullity}(T)$.

---

## Main proof

<Proof>
Let $\dim V = n$, $\text{nullity}(T) = k$, and let $\{u_1, \ldots, u_k\}$ be a basis for $\ker(T)$.

By the basis extension theorem, extend to a basis for $V$:

$$\{u_1, \ldots, u_k, w_1, \ldots, w_{n-k}\}$$

is a basis for $V$, where $n - k = \dim V - \text{nullity}(T)$.

**Claim**: $\{T(w_1), \ldots, T(w_{n-k})\}$ is a basis for $\text{im}(T)$.

*Spanning*: Let $y \in \text{im}(T)$. Then $y = T(v)$ for some $v \in V$. Write $v = a_1 u_1 + \cdots + a_k u_k + b_1 w_1 + \cdots + b_{n-k} w_{n-k}$. Then

$$y = T(v) = a_1 T(u_1) + \cdots + a_k T(u_k) + b_1 T(w_1) + \cdots + b_{n-k} T(w_{n-k}).$$

Since $u_i \in \ker(T)$, $T(u_i) = 0$. So $y = b_1 T(w_1) + \cdots + b_{n-k} T(w_{n-k})$.

*Independence*: Suppose $c_1 T(w_1) + \cdots + c_{n-k} T(w_{n-k}) = 0$. Then

$$T(c_1 w_1 + \cdots + c_{n-k} w_{n-k}) = 0,$$

so $c_1 w_1 + \cdots + c_{n-k} w_{n-k} \in \ker(T)$. Write this as a linear combination of the kernel basis:

$$c_1 w_1 + \cdots + c_{n-k} w_{n-k} = d_1 u_1 + \cdots + d_k u_k.$$

Rearranging: $d_1 u_1 + \cdots + d_k u_k - c_1 w_1 - \cdots - c_{n-k} w_{n-k} = 0$. Since $\{u_1, \ldots, u_k, w_1, \ldots, w_{n-k}\}$ is a basis for $V$ (hence linearly independent), all coefficients are zero. In particular, $c_1 = \cdots = c_{n-k} = 0$.

Therefore $\{T(w_1), \ldots, T(w_{n-k})\}$ is a basis for $\text{im}(T)$, so

$$\text{rank}(T) = n - k = \dim V - \text{nullity}(T).$$

Rearranging: $\dim V = \text{rank}(T) + \text{nullity}(T)$.
</Proof>

## Worked examples illustrating the proof

<Example id="trace-concrete" title="Trace: concrete illustration">
$T = \text{tr} : M_{2 \times 2}(\mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R}$, $\dim V = 4$.

**Step 1**: $\ker(T) = \{A \mid a_{11} + a_{22} = 0\}$. Basis for kernel:

$$u_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix},\; u_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix},\; u_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.$$

So $k = 3$.

**Step 2**: Extend to a basis of $M_{2 \times 2}(\mathbb{R})$ by adding $w_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Step 3**: $\{T(w_1)\} = \{1\}$ is a basis for $\text{im}(T) = \mathbb{R}$.

So rank $= 1 = 4 - 3$. Check: $4 = 1 + 3$.
</Example>

<Example id="derivative-concrete" title="Derivative: concrete illustration">
$D : P_2(\mathbb{R}) \to P_1(\mathbb{R})$, $\dim V = 3$.

$\ker(D) = \text{span}\{1\}$, $k = 1$.

Extend: $\{1, x, x^2\}$ is a basis for $P_2(\mathbb{R})$, with $u_1 = 1$, $w_1 = x$, $w_2 = x^2$.

$\{D(x), D(x^2)\} = \{1, 2x\}$ is a basis for $P_1(\mathbb{R}) = \text{im}(D)$.

rank $= 2 = 3 - 1$. Check: $3 = 2 + 1$.
</Example>

<Example id="projection-concrete" title="Projection: concrete illustration">
$T : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^2$, $T(x, y, z) = (x, y)$, $\dim V = 3$.

$\ker(T) = \text{span}\{e_3\}$, $k = 1$.

Extend: $\{e_3, e_1, e_2\}$ is a basis for $\mathbb{R}^3$.

$\{T(e_1), T(e_2)\} = \{(1,0), (0,1)\}$ is a basis for $\mathbb{R}^2 = \text{im}(T)$.

rank $= 2 = 3 - 1$. Check: $3 = 2 + 1$.
</Example>

<Example id="injection-concrete" title="Injection: trivial kernel">
$T : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3$, $T(x, y) = (x, y, 0)$, $\dim V = 2$.

$\ker(T) = \{0\}$, $k = 0$. No kernel basis to extend.

$\{T(e_1), T(e_2)\} = \{(1,0,0), (0,1,0)\}$ is a basis for $\text{im}(T)$.

rank $= 2 = 2 - 0$. Check: $2 = 2 + 0$.
</Example>

<Example id="singular-matrix" title="Matrix with nontrivial kernel">
$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$, $T_A : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$, $\dim V = 2$.

$\ker(T_A) = \text{span}\{(-2, 1)\}$, $k = 1$.

Extend: $\{(-2, 1), (1, 0)\}$ is a basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$.

$\{T_A(1, 0)\} = \{(1, 2)\}$ is a basis for $\text{im}(T_A) = \text{span}\{(1, 2)\}$ (the column space).

rank $= 1 = 2 - 1$. Check: $2 = 1 + 1$.
</Example>

<Example id="proof-structure" title="Structure of the proof">
The proof strategy is beautifully simple:

1. Find a basis for $\ker(T)$ (size $k$).
2. Extend to a basis for all of $V$ (total size $n$, added $n - k$ vectors).
3. Show the images of the added vectors form a basis for $\text{im}(T)$.

The kernel vectors contribute nothing to the image (they map to $0$), while the remaining vectors map to an independent spanning set.
</Example>

<Example id="short-exact" title="Short exact sequence viewpoint">
The Rank-Nullity Theorem says the sequence

$$0 \to \ker(T) \xrightarrow{\iota} V \xrightarrow{T} \text{im}(T) \to 0$$

is a **short exact sequence** of vector spaces. The dimension formula $\dim V = \dim \ker(T) + \dim \text{im}(T)$ is the statement that dimensions are additive across short exact sequences. This generalizes to the theory of [exact sequences in abelian categories](/category-theory/ch03-abelian-categories/concepts/c04-exact-sequence).
</Example>

<Example id="quotient-viewpoint" title="Quotient space viewpoint">
The first isomorphism theorem gives $V/\ker(T) \cong \text{im}(T)$. Therefore:

$$\dim V = \dim \ker(T) + \dim(V/\ker(T)) = \dim \ker(T) + \dim \text{im}(T).$$

This is perhaps the most conceptual proof of the Rank-Nullity Theorem.
</Example>

<Example id="application-existence" title="Application: existence of solutions">
Does $T(x, y, z) = (x + y, y + z, x + z)$ satisfy $T(v) = (1, 2, 3)$ for some $v$?

$\text{rank}(T)$: the matrix is $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ with $\det = 2 \neq 0$. So rank $= 3 = \dim \mathbb{R}^3$, meaning $T$ is surjective. Yes, a solution exists (and it is unique since nullity $= 0$).
</Example>

<Example id="matrix-rank" title="Row rank equals column rank">
For a matrix $A \in M_{m \times n}(F)$, the Rank-Nullity Theorem applied to $T_A$ gives rank$(A) = n - $ nullity$(A)$. Here rank$(A)$ is the column rank. A separate argument shows **row rank = column rank**, giving a single notion of "rank."
</Example>

<Example id="pigeonhole" title="Linear algebra pigeonhole">
If $T : F^5 \to F^3$ is linear, then nullity$(T) \geq 5 - 3 = 2$. So $\ker(T)$ is at least 2-dimensional. This "pigeonhole principle for linear maps" is a consequence of Rank-Nullity.
</Example>

<Example id="systems-summary" title="Summary for linear systems">
For $Ax = b$ with $A \in M_{m \times n}(F)$:

- **Existence**: $b \in C(A)$ iff rank$(A) = $ rank$(A | b)$.
- **Uniqueness**: the solution is unique iff nullity$(A) = 0$, i.e., rank$(A) = n$.
- **Number of free variables**: $n - $ rank$(A)$.
</Example>

---

<Remark title="Summary">
The Rank-Nullity Theorem is the fundamental balance equation of linear algebra: the "input dimension" splits into "information preserved" (rank) plus "information lost" (nullity). This principle permeates all of linear algebra and extends to exact sequences in homological algebra.
</Remark>
