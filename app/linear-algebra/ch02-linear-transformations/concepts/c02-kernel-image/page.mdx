# Kernel and Image

The **kernel** (null space) and **image** (range) of a linear transformation are the two fundamental subspaces associated with it. They measure how much information the map "destroys" and "covers."

---

## Definitions

<Definition title="Kernel (null space)" number="2.2">
Let $T : V \to W$ be a linear transformation. The **kernel** (or **null space**) of $T$ is

$$\ker(T) = N(T) = \{v \in V \mid T(v) = 0\}.$$
</Definition>

<Definition title="Image (range)" number="2.3">
The **image** (or **range**) of $T$ is

$$\text{im}(T) = R(T) = \{T(v) \mid v \in V\} = \{w \in W \mid w = T(v) \text{ for some } v \in V\}.$$
</Definition>

<Definition title="Rank and nullity" number="2.4">
The **rank** of $T$ is $\text{rank}(T) = \dim(\text{im}(T))$.

The **nullity** of $T$ is $\text{nullity}(T) = \dim(\ker(T))$.
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Kernel and image are subspaces" number="2.2">
For $T : V \to W$ linear:
1. $\ker(T)$ is a subspace of $V$.
2. $\text{im}(T)$ is a subspace of $W$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
For (1): $T(0) = 0$, so $0 \in \ker(T)$. If $T(u) = 0$ and $T(v) = 0$, then $T(au + bv) = aT(u) + bT(v) = 0$, so $au + bv \in \ker(T)$.

For (2): $T(0) = 0 \in \text{im}(T)$. If $w_1 = T(v_1)$ and $w_2 = T(v_2)$, then $aw_1 + bw_2 = T(av_1 + bv_2) \in \text{im}(T)$.
</Proof>

## Examples

<Example id="matrix-kernel" title="Kernel of a matrix transformation">
For $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$, $T_A : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ with $T_A(x) = Ax$.

Row reducing: $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & -3 & -6 \end{pmatrix} \to \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

So $\ker(T_A) = \{t(1, -2, 1) \mid t \in \mathbb{R}\}$. Nullity $= 1$, rank $= 2$.
</Example>

<Example id="derivative-kernel" title="Kernel of differentiation">
$D : P_n(\mathbb{R}) \to P_{n-1}(\mathbb{R})$, $D(p) = p'$.

$\ker(D) = \{p \in P_n(\mathbb{R}) \mid p' = 0\} = \{c \mid c \in \mathbb{R}\}$ (the constant polynomials).

So $\text{nullity}(D) = 1$ and $\text{im}(D) = P_{n-1}(\mathbb{R})$, giving $\text{rank}(D) = n$.
</Example>

<Example id="projection-kernel" title="Kernel and image of a projection">
$\pi : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^2$, $\pi(x, y, z) = (x, y)$.

$\ker(\pi) = \{(0, 0, z) \mid z \in \mathbb{R}\}$ (the $z$-axis), nullity $= 1$.

$\text{im}(\pi) = \mathbb{R}^2$, rank $= 2$.
</Example>

<Example id="trace-kernel" title="Kernel of the trace">
$\text{tr} : M_{2 \times 2}(F) \to F$, $\text{tr}\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = a + d$.

$\ker(\text{tr}) = \{A \mid a + d = 0\}$, the trace-zero matrices. This has dimension $3$ (nullity $= 3$).

$\text{im}(\text{tr}) = F$, rank $= 1$.

Check: $3 + 1 = 4 = \dim M_{2 \times 2}(F)$.
</Example>

<Example id="rotation-kernel" title="Kernel of rotation">
$R_\theta : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ (rotation by $\theta \neq 0$).

$\ker(R_\theta) = \{0\}$ (only the origin is fixed), nullity $= 0$.

$\text{im}(R_\theta) = \mathbb{R}^2$ (rotation is surjective), rank $= 2$.
</Example>

<Example id="zero-map-kernel" title="Kernel of the zero map">
$T_0 : V \to W$, $T_0(v) = 0$ for all $v$.

$\ker(T_0) = V$, nullity $= \dim V$.

$\text{im}(T_0) = \{0\}$, rank $= 0$.
</Example>

<Example id="identity-kernel" title="Kernel of the identity">
$\text{id}_V : V \to V$, $\text{id}_V(v) = v$.

$\ker(\text{id}_V) = \{0\}$, nullity $= 0$.

$\text{im}(\text{id}_V) = V$, rank $= \dim V$.
</Example>

<Example id="transpose-kernel" title="Kernel and image of transpose">
$T : M_{2 \times 2}(F) \to M_{2 \times 2}(F)$, $T(A) = A^T$.

$\ker(T) = \{0\}$ (since $A^T = 0$ implies $A = 0$), nullity $= 0$.

$\text{im}(T) = M_{2 \times 2}(F)$ (since $(B^T)^T = B$), rank $= 4$.
</Example>

<Example id="homogeneous-system" title="Null space of a matrix">
For any $A \in M_{m \times n}(F)$, $N(A) = \ker(T_A)$ is the solution set of $Ax = 0$. The **column space** $C(A) = \text{im}(T_A)$ is spanned by the columns of $A$.
</Example>

<Example id="integration-kernel" title="Kernel of definite integration">
$T : C[-1, 1] \to \mathbb{R}$, $T(f) = \int_{-1}^1 f(x)\,dx$.

$\ker(T)$ is the set of continuous functions with zero integral over $[-1, 1]$. For instance, all odd functions belong to $\ker(T)$. This kernel is infinite-dimensional.
</Example>

<Example id="eval-kernel" title="Kernel of evaluation">
$\text{ev}_c : P_n(F) \to F$, $\text{ev}_c(p) = p(c)$.

$\ker(\text{ev}_c) = \{p \in P_n(F) \mid p(c) = 0\}$, i.e., polynomials with root at $c$. These are of the form $(x - c)q(x)$ with $\deg q \leq n - 1$, so nullity $= n$.
</Example>

<Example id="left-shift-kernel" title="Kernel of the left shift">
$L : F^\infty \to F^\infty$, $L(a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots) = (a_2, a_3, a_4, \ldots)$.

$\ker(L) = \{(a_1, 0, 0, \ldots) \mid a_1 \in F\}$, which is 1-dimensional.

$\text{im}(L) = F^\infty$ (surjective: for any $(b_1, b_2, \ldots)$, take $(0, b_1, b_2, \ldots)$).
</Example>

## Injectivity and surjectivity

<Theorem title="Kernel characterizes injectivity" number="2.3">
A linear map $T : V \to W$ is **injective** (one-to-one) if and only if $\ker(T) = \{0\}$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
($\Rightarrow$) If $T$ is injective and $T(v) = 0 = T(0)$, then $v = 0$.

($\Leftarrow$) If $\ker(T) = \{0\}$ and $T(u) = T(v)$, then $T(u - v) = 0$, so $u - v = 0$, i.e., $u = v$.
</Proof>

<Theorem title="Image characterizes surjectivity" number="2.4">
$T : V \to W$ is **surjective** (onto) if and only if $\text{im}(T) = W$.
</Theorem>

<Remark title="Looking ahead">
The precise relationship between kernel and image is given by the [Rank-Nullity Theorem](/linear-algebra/ch02-linear-transformations/theorems/t01-rank-nullity): $\dim V = \text{rank}(T) + \text{nullity}(T)$. A linear map that is both injective and surjective is an [isomorphism](/linear-algebra/ch02-linear-transformations/concepts/c03-isomorphism).
</Remark>
