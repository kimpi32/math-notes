# Inverse Matrices

A square matrix is **invertible** if there exists another matrix that "undoes" its effect. Invertibility is the matrix analogue of bijectivity for linear maps.

---

## Definition

<Definition title="Invertible matrix" number="3.5">
A square matrix $A \in M_{n \times n}(F)$ is **invertible** (or **nonsingular**) if there exists a matrix $B \in M_{n \times n}(F)$ such that

$$AB = BA = I_n.$$

The matrix $B$ is called the **inverse** of $A$ and is denoted $A^{-1}$. If no such $B$ exists, $A$ is **singular** (non-invertible).
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Uniqueness of inverse" number="3.1">
If $A$ is invertible, then $A^{-1}$ is unique.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
If $AB = I$ and $AC = I$, then $B = IB = (CA)B = C(AB) = CI = C$.
</Proof>

<Remark title="One-sided inverses suffice">
For square matrices, if $AB = I$, then automatically $BA = I$ as well (this follows from the Rank-Nullity Theorem: $AB = I$ implies $B$ is injective, hence bijective, hence $B$ has a left inverse, which must be $A$). This fails for non-square matrices and for infinite-dimensional operators.
</Remark>

## Examples

<Example id="2x2-inverse" title="Inverse of a 2 x 2 matrix">
For $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$ with $ad - bc \neq 0$:

$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}.$$

For example, $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 5 & 3 \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ -5 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ since $\det = 6 - 5 = 1$.
</Example>

<Example id="diagonal-inverse" title="Inverse of a diagonal matrix">
$\text{diag}(d_1, \ldots, d_n)^{-1} = \text{diag}(1/d_1, \ldots, 1/d_n)$, provided all $d_i \neq 0$. If any $d_i = 0$, the matrix is singular.
</Example>

<Example id="identity-inverse" title="Identity is its own inverse">
$I_n^{-1} = I_n$.
</Example>

<Example id="singular-example" title="A singular matrix">
$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$ is singular: $\det A = 4 - 4 = 0$. Equivalently, the columns $(1, 2)^T$ and $(2, 4)^T$ are linearly dependent, so $A$ has a nontrivial kernel: $A(-2, 1)^T = (0, 0)^T$.
</Example>

<Example id="rotation-inverse" title="Inverse of a rotation matrix">
$R_\theta = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$ has inverse $R_{-\theta} = R_\theta^T = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$.

Rotation matrices are **orthogonal**: $R^T R = I$, so $R^{-1} = R^T$.
</Example>

<Example id="upper-triangular-inverse" title="Inverse of an upper triangular matrix">
An upper triangular matrix is invertible iff all diagonal entries are nonzero. Its inverse is also upper triangular:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2/3 \\ 0 & 1/3 \end{pmatrix}.$$
</Example>

<Example id="elementary-inverses" title="Elementary matrices are invertible">
Elementary matrices (corresponding to elementary row operations) are invertible:

- Row swap: $(E_{ij})^{-1} = E_{ij}$ (swapping twice returns to original).
- Row scaling by $c \neq 0$: inverse scales by $1/c$.
- Adding $c$ times row $j$ to row $i$: inverse subtracts $c$ times row $j$ from row $i$.
</Example>

<Example id="product-inverse" title="Inverse of a product">
$(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$ (order reverses, like taking off socks and shoes).

$(ABC)^{-1} = C^{-1}B^{-1}A^{-1}$.
</Example>

<Example id="transpose-inverse" title="Inverse of a transpose">
$(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$. Proof: $A^T (A^{-1})^T = (A^{-1}A)^T = I^T = I$.
</Example>

<Example id="powers-inverse" title="Inverse of a power">
$(A^k)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^k$, often written $A^{-k}$.
</Example>

<Example id="gauss-jordan" title="Computing the inverse by Gauss-Jordan">
To find $A^{-1}$, augment $[A \mid I]$ and row-reduce to $[I \mid A^{-1}]$.

Starting from $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 7 \end{pmatrix}$: apply $R_2 \to R_2 - 3R_1$ to get pivots, then $R_1 \to R_1 - 2R_2$ to reach RREF. The right half of the augmented matrix becomes $A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

Verify: $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 7 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 7 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$.
</Example>

<Example id="matrix-equation" title="Solving Ax = b via inverse">
If $A$ is invertible, the unique solution to $Ax = b$ is $x = A^{-1}b$.

$$\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 5 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \implies \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ -5 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}.$$
</Example>

## The general linear group

<Definition title="General linear group" number="3.6">
The set of all invertible $n \times n$ matrices over $F$ forms a group under matrix multiplication:

$$\text{GL}_n(F) = \{A \in M_{n \times n}(F) \mid A \text{ is invertible}\}.$$

This is the **general linear group**. It is a non-abelian group for $n \geq 2$.
</Definition>

<Example id="gl2" title="Elements of GL_2(R)">
$\text{GL}_2(\mathbb{R}) = \{A \in M_{2 \times 2}(\mathbb{R}) \mid ad - bc \neq 0\}$. This is an open, dense subset of $M_{2 \times 2}(\mathbb{R}) \cong \mathbb{R}^4$, with the singular matrices forming a hypersurface of measure zero.
</Example>

</Remark>
