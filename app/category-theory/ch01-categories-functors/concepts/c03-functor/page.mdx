# Functor

A functor is a "morphism of categories" — it maps objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms, preserving the categorical structure (composition and identities). Functors are the primary tool for relating different mathematical structures.

---

## Definition

<Definition title="Covariant Functor" number="1.7">
A **(covariant) functor** $F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ between categories consists of:

1. A map on objects: for each $A \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$, an object $F(A) \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{D})$.
2. A map on morphisms: for each morphism $f : A \to B$ in $\mathcal{C}$, a morphism $F(f) : F(A) \to F(B)$ in $\mathcal{D}$.

subject to:

- **Preservation of identity:** $F(\mathrm{id}_A) = \mathrm{id}_{F(A)}$ for all $A$.
- **Preservation of composition:** $F(g \circ f) = F(g) \circ F(f)$ for all composable pairs $f, g$.
</Definition>

<Definition title="Contravariant Functor" number="1.8">
A **contravariant functor** $F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ is a covariant functor $F : \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathcal{D}$. Equivalently, it reverses the direction of morphisms: $f : A \to B$ maps to $F(f) : F(B) \to F(A)$, and

$$F(g \circ f) = F(f) \circ F(g)$$
</Definition>

<Remark title="Modern convention">
In modern usage, "functor" always means "covariant functor." A "contravariant functor $\mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$" is simply a functor $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathcal{D}$.
</Remark>

---

## Faithful, Full, and Essentially Surjective

<Definition title="Faithful, Full, Fully Faithful" number="1.9">
A functor $F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ is:

- **Faithful** if for all $A, B \in \mathcal{C}$, the map $F_{A,B} : \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B) \to \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(A), F(B))$ is injective.
- **Full** if each $F_{A,B}$ is surjective.
- **Fully faithful** if each $F_{A,B}$ is bijective.
- **Essentially surjective** if for every $D \in \mathcal{D}$, there exists $C \in \mathcal{C}$ with $F(C) \cong D$.
</Definition>

<Remark title="Equivalence via functors">
A functor is an [equivalence of categories](/category-theory/ch01-categories-functors/concepts/c05-equivalence) if and only if it is fully faithful and essentially surjective. This is a fundamental characterization.
</Remark>

## Examples

<Example title="Forgetful functors" id="forgetful">
The **forgetful functor** $U : \mathbf{Grp} \to \mathbf{Set}$ sends each group to its underlying set and each homomorphism to the underlying function. Similarly:

- $U : \mathbf{Top} \to \mathbf{Set}$ forgets the topology.
- $U : R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod} \to \mathbf{Ab}$ forgets the $R$-module structure, retaining only the abelian group.
- $U : \mathbf{Ab} \to \mathbf{Grp}$ is the inclusion (every abelian group is a group).

Forgetful functors are typically faithful but not full.
</Example>

<Example title="Free functors" id="free">
The **free functor** $F : \mathbf{Set} \to \mathbf{Grp}$ sends a set $S$ to the free group $F(S)$ generated by $S$. More generally, free constructions are left adjoint to forgetful functors. For instance:

- $\mathbf{Set} \to \mathbf{Ab}$: $S \mapsto \mathbb{Z}^{(S)}$ (free abelian group).
- $\mathbf{Set} \to R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$: $S \mapsto R^{(S)}$ (free module).
- $\mathbf{Set} \to \mathbf{Ring}$: $S \mapsto \mathbb{Z}[x_s : s \in S]$ (polynomial ring).
</Example>

<Example title="Hom functors" id="hom">
For a locally small category $\mathcal{C}$ and a fixed object $A$:

- **Covariant Hom:** $\mathrm{Hom}(A, -) : \mathcal{C} \to \mathbf{Set}$ sends $B \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}(A,B)$ and $f \mapsto f_* = f \circ -$.
- **Contravariant Hom:** $\mathrm{Hom}(-, A) : \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$ sends $B \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}(B,A)$ and $f \mapsto f^* = - \circ f$.

These are the [representable functors](/category-theory/ch01-categories-functors/concepts/c06-representable) central to the [Yoneda Lemma](/category-theory/ch01-categories-functors/theorems/t01-yoneda).
</Example>

<Example title="Power set functor" id="powerset">
The **covariant power set functor** $\mathcal{P} : \mathbf{Set} \to \mathbf{Set}$ sends $A \mapsto \mathcal{P}(A)$ (the power set) and $f : A \to B$ to $f_* : \mathcal{P}(A) \to \mathcal{P}(B)$ by $f_*(S) = f(S)$ (direct image).

The **contravariant power set functor** $\mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{op}} : \mathbf{Set}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$ sends $f : A \to B$ to $f^{-1} : \mathcal{P}(B) \to \mathcal{P}(A)$ by $f^{-1}(T) = \{a \in A : f(a) \in T\}$ (preimage).
</Example>

<Example title="Fundamental group functor" id="pi1">
The functor $\pi_1 : \mathbf{Top}_* \to \mathbf{Grp}$ sends a pointed topological space $(X, x_0)$ to its fundamental group $\pi_1(X, x_0)$ and a basepoint-preserving continuous map $f$ to the induced homomorphism $f_*$. The functoriality $(g \circ f)_* = g_* \circ f_*$ is the key to why homotopy-equivalent spaces have isomorphic fundamental groups.
</Example>

<Example title="Homology as a functor" id="homology">
For each $n \geq 0$, singular homology gives a functor $H_n : \mathbf{Top} \to \mathbf{Ab}$. It sends a continuous map $f : X \to Y$ to the induced map $f_* : H_n(X) \to H_n(Y)$. This is a covariant functor. The functoriality encodes the fact that homotopic maps induce the same map on homology.
</Example>

<Example title="Tangent bundle functor" id="tangent">
The functor $T : \mathbf{Man} \to \mathbf{VectBdl}$ sends a smooth manifold $M$ to its tangent bundle $TM$ and a smooth map $f : M \to N$ to its differential $df : TM \to TN$. The chain rule $d(g \circ f) = dg \circ df$ is precisely the functoriality condition.
</Example>

<Example title="Dual space functor" id="dual">
The **dual space functor** $(-)^* : \mathbf{Vect}_k \to \mathbf{Vect}_k^{\mathrm{op}}$ sends a vector space $V$ to $V^* = \mathrm{Hom}_k(V, k)$ and a linear map $T : V \to W$ to its transpose $T^* : W^* \to V^*$. This is a contravariant functor: it reverses arrows. On finite-dimensional spaces, double dualization $(-)^{**}$ is naturally isomorphic to the identity functor.
</Example>

<Example title="Abelianization functor" id="abelianization">
The **abelianization** functor $\mathrm{ab} : \mathbf{Grp} \to \mathbf{Ab}$ sends $G$ to $G^{\mathrm{ab}} = G/[G,G]$, where $[G,G]$ is the commutator subgroup. This is left adjoint to the inclusion functor $\mathbf{Ab} \hookrightarrow \mathbf{Grp}$: there is a natural bijection $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Ab}}(G^{\mathrm{ab}}, A) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Grp}}(G, A)$ for abelian $A$.
</Example>

<Example title="Presheaf as a functor" id="presheaf">
For a topological space $X$, a **presheaf** of abelian groups on $X$ is a contravariant functor $\mathcal{F} : \mathbf{Open}(X)^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Ab}$. The assignment $U \mapsto \mathcal{F}(U)$ is the "sections over $U$," and for $V \subseteq U$ the restriction map $\mathcal{F}(U) \to \mathcal{F}(V)$ is the image of the inclusion $V \hookrightarrow U$.
</Example>

<Example title="Spec and the global sections functors" id="spec">
In algebraic geometry, $\mathrm{Spec} : \mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Sch}$ is a contravariant functor from commutative rings to schemes. Its adjoint is the global sections functor $\Gamma : \mathbf{Sch} \to \mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}}$. For affine schemes, $\Gamma(\mathrm{Spec}\, A, \mathcal{O}) \cong A$, recovering the ring from the scheme.
</Example>

<Example title="Identity and composition of functors" id="composition">
For any category $\mathcal{C}$, the **identity functor** $\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{C}} : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}$ sends every object and morphism to itself. Given functors $F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ and $G : \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{E}$, the **composition** $G \circ F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{E}$ is defined by $(G \circ F)(A) = G(F(A))$ and $(G \circ F)(f) = G(F(f))$. This makes $\mathbf{Cat}$ (the category of small categories) into a category.
</Example>

## Functors Preserve Isomorphisms

<Theorem title="Functors preserve isomorphisms" number="1.5">
If $F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ is a functor and $f : A \to B$ is an isomorphism in $\mathcal{C}$, then $F(f) : F(A) \to F(B)$ is an isomorphism in $\mathcal{D}$ with inverse $F(f^{-1})$.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
$F(f) \circ F(f^{-1}) = F(f \circ f^{-1}) = F(\mathrm{id}_B) = \mathrm{id}_{F(B)}$, and similarly $F(f^{-1}) \circ F(f) = \mathrm{id}_{F(A)}$.
</Proof>

<Remark title="Functors need not preserve mono or epi">
While functors always preserve isomorphisms, a general functor need not preserve monomorphisms or epimorphisms. However, left adjoint functors preserve epimorphisms (and all colimits), and right adjoint functors preserve monomorphisms (and all limits). This is one of the key applications of [adjoint functors](/category-theory/ch02-limits-colimits/concepts/c05-adjunction).
</Remark>
