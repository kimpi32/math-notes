# Category

A category is the most basic structure in category theory. It captures the idea of "objects with structure-preserving maps between them" at the highest level of abstraction.

<Definition title="Category" number="1.1">
A **category** $\mathcal{C}$ consists of the following data:

1. A collection $\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$ of **objects**.
2. For every pair of objects $A, B$, a collection $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A, B)$ of **morphisms** (or arrows) from $A$ to $B$.
3. For every triple $A, B, C$, a **composition** map

$$\circ : \mathrm{Hom}(B, C) \times \mathrm{Hom}(A, B) \to \mathrm{Hom}(A, C), \qquad (g, f) \mapsto g \circ f$$

4. For every object $A$, an **identity morphism** $\mathrm{id}_A \in \mathrm{Hom}(A, A)$.

subject to the axioms:

- **Associativity.** For $f: A \to B$, $g: B \to C$, $h: C \to D$:

$$h \circ (g \circ f) = (h \circ g) \circ f$$

- **Identity.** For $f : A \to B$:

$$f \circ \mathrm{id}_A = f = \mathrm{id}_B \circ f$$
</Definition>

<Definition title="Locally small category" number="1.2">
A category $\mathcal{C}$ is **locally small** if for every pair of objects $A, B$, the collection $\mathrm{Hom}(A, B)$ is a set (not a proper class).

A category is **small** if both $\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$ and all $\mathrm{Hom}(A,B)$ are sets.
</Definition>

<Remark title="Size issues">
The distinction between "small" and "large" categories matters. The category of all sets cannot have a set of objects (Russell's paradox). Most categories we work with are locally small but not small. We will be casual about set-theoretic issues, following Gelfand–Manin's convention: assume a fixed Grothendieck universe when needed.
</Remark>

## The Zoo: Examples of Categories

### Concrete categories (objects = sets with structure)

<Example id="set" title="Set — the category of sets">
- **Objects:** All sets.
- **Morphisms:** $\mathrm{Hom}(A, B)$ = all functions $f: A \to B$.
- **Composition:** Ordinary function composition.
- **Identity:** The identity function $\mathrm{id}_A(x) = x$.

$\mathbf{Set}$ is in some sense the "mother of all categories." The Yoneda embedding sends every category into a category of $\mathbf{Set}$-valued functors.
</Example>

<Example id="grp" title="Grp — the category of groups">
- **Objects:** All groups.
- **Morphisms:** Group homomorphisms $\varphi : G \to H$ (maps preserving the group operation).
- **Composition:** Composition of homomorphisms.
- **Identity:** The identity homomorphism.

Note: $\mathrm{Hom}(G, H)$ is just a set here — it does not naturally have a group structure (unless $H$ is abelian).
</Example>

<Example id="ab" title="Ab — the category of abelian groups">
- **Objects:** Abelian groups.
- **Morphisms:** Group homomorphisms.

Unlike $\mathbf{Grp}$, this is an **additive category**: each $\mathrm{Hom}(A, B)$ is itself an abelian group (under pointwise addition), and composition is bilinear. This makes $\mathbf{Ab}$ an **abelian category** — the natural home for homological algebra.
</Example>

<Example id="top" title="Top — the category of topological spaces">
- **Objects:** Topological spaces $(X, \tau)$.
- **Morphisms:** Continuous maps.

Variants: $\mathbf{Top}_*$ (pointed spaces, basepoint-preserving maps), $\mathbf{Haus}$ (Hausdorff spaces).
</Example>

<Example id="rmod" title="R-Mod — the category of modules">
Fix a ring $R$.

- **Objects:** Left $R$-modules.
- **Morphisms:** $R$-module homomorphisms.

When $R = k$ is a field, $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod} = \mathbf{Vect}_k$, the category of $k$-vector spaces. This is the most important abelian category in linear algebra.

When $R = \mathbb{Z}$, $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod} = \mathbf{Ab}$.
</Example>

### Algebraic / order-theoretic categories

<Example id="poset" title="A poset as a category">
Any **partially ordered set** $(P, \leq)$ defines a category:

- **Objects:** Elements of $P$.
- **Morphisms:** There is exactly one morphism $x \to y$ if $x \leq y$, and none otherwise.

$$\mathrm{Hom}(x, y) = \begin{cases} \{*\} & \text{if } x \leq y \\ \varnothing & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

- **Composition:** Transitivity of $\leq$.
- **Identity:** Reflexivity of $\leq$.

This means: **functors from poset categories = order-preserving maps**. Many constructions in order theory (meets, joins) become special cases of categorical limits and colimits.
</Example>

<Example id="bg" title="A group as a category">
Any **group** $G$ defines a category $\mathbf{B}G$ with:

- **Objects:** A single object $*$.
- **Morphisms:** $\mathrm{Hom}(*, *) = G$.
- **Composition:** The group operation.
- **Identity:** The group identity $e$.

The invertibility of all morphisms makes $\mathbf{B}G$ a **groupoid** (a category where every morphism is an isomorphism). A functor $\mathbf{B}G \to \mathbf{Set}$ is exactly a **$G$-set** (a set with a $G$-action). This is why the Yoneda lemma specializes to Cayley's theorem for groups.
</Example>

<Example id="monoid" title="A monoid as a category">
More generally, any **monoid** $(M, \cdot, e)$ defines a one-object category:

- **Objects:** $\{*\}$.
- **Morphisms:** $\mathrm{Hom}(*, *) = M$.
- **Composition:** The monoid operation.

A group is a monoid where every element is invertible — hence the previous example. A functor from a monoid category to $\mathbf{Set}$ is an **$M$-action** on a set.
</Example>

### Categories from other categories

<Example id="opposite" title="The opposite category">
For any category $\mathcal{C}$, the **opposite category** $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ has:

- **Objects:** Same as $\mathcal{C}$.
- **Morphisms:** $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}}(A, B) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B, A)$ — all arrows reversed.

This is a powerful trick: any theorem about categories immediately gives a "dual" theorem by passing to $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$. For instance, a product in $\mathcal{C}$ is a coproduct in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$.
</Example>

<Example id="product" title="The product category">
Given categories $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{D}$, the **product category** $\mathcal{C} \times \mathcal{D}$ has:

- **Objects:** Pairs $(C, D)$ with $C \in \mathcal{C}$, $D \in \mathcal{D}$.
- **Morphisms:** $\mathrm{Hom}((C,D),(C',D')) = \mathrm{Hom}_\mathcal{C}(C,C') \times \mathrm{Hom}_\mathcal{D}(D,D')$.
- **Composition:** Componentwise.
</Example>

<Example id="functor-cat" title="The functor category">
For categories $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{D}$, the **functor category** $[\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{D}]$ (also written $\mathcal{D}^{\mathcal{C}}$) has:

- **Objects:** Functors $F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$.
- **Morphisms:** Natural transformations $\alpha : F \Rightarrow G$.
- **Composition:** Vertical composition of natural transformations.

The presheaf category $[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}, \mathbf{Set}]$ is the target of the Yoneda embedding. It is always complete and cocomplete, even if $\mathcal{C}$ is not.
</Example>

### Geometric / topological categories

<Example id="rel" title="Rel — the category of relations">
- **Objects:** Sets.
- **Morphisms:** $\mathrm{Hom}(A, B)$ = all relations $R \subseteq A \times B$.
- **Composition:** Relational composition. Given $R \subseteq A \times B$ and $S \subseteq B \times C$:

$$S \circ R = \{(a, c) \mid \exists\, b \in B : (a,b) \in R \text{ and } (b,c) \in S\}$$

- **Identity:** The diagonal relation $\Delta_A = \{(a,a) \mid a \in A\}$.

$\mathbf{Set}$ is a subcategory of $\mathbf{Rel}$ (every function is a relation). But $\mathbf{Rel}$ is richer: morphisms are not required to be functions. Note that $\mathbf{Rel}$ is **self-dual**: $\mathbf{Rel} \cong \mathbf{Rel}^{\mathrm{op}}$ via $(a,b) \mapsto (b,a)$.
</Example>

<Example id="mat" title="Mat_k — the matrix category">
Fix a field $k$.

- **Objects:** Natural numbers $0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$
- **Morphisms:** $\mathrm{Hom}(m, n)$ = all $n \times m$ matrices with entries in $k$.
- **Composition:** Matrix multiplication.
- **Identity:** The $n \times n$ identity matrix $I_n$.

This is a **skeleton** of $\mathbf{Vect}_k^{\mathrm{fd}}$ (finite-dimensional vector spaces): every finite-dimensional vector space is isomorphic to exactly one object $n$ in $\mathbf{Mat}_k$. The fact that matrix multiplication is associative is precisely the category axiom — and it encodes all of linear algebra.
</Example>

<Example id="man" title="Smooth manifolds and more">
- $\mathbf{Man}$: smooth manifolds with smooth maps.
- $\mathbf{Diff}$: smooth manifolds with diffeomorphisms only (a groupoid).

The functor $\mathbf{Man} \to \mathbf{Vect}_{\mathbb{R}}$ sending $M \mapsto T_p M$ (tangent space at a point) is a key example in differential geometry. The chain rule $D(g \circ f)_p = Dg_{f(p)} \circ Df_p$ is precisely the statement that differentiation is a **functor**.
</Example>

<Example id="open" title="Open sets of a topological space">
For a topological space $X$, define a category $\mathbf{Open}(X)$:

- **Objects:** Open sets $U \subseteq X$.
- **Morphisms:** Inclusions $U \hookrightarrow V$ (at most one morphism per pair).
- **Composition:** Transitivity of inclusion.

This is a poset category. A **presheaf** on $X$ is a contravariant functor $\mathbf{Open}(X)^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$ (or to $\mathbf{Ab}$, $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$, etc.). Sheaves, the foundation of algebraic geometry, are presheaves satisfying a gluing condition. The category $\mathbf{Sh}(X)$ of abelian sheaves on $X$ is an abelian category — and its derived category is the starting point for sheaf cohomology.
</Example>

### Combinatorial / categorical categories

<Example id="delta" title="The simplex category Δ">
The **simplex category** $\mathbf{\Delta}$ has:

- **Objects:** Finite totally ordered sets $[n] = \{0, 1, \ldots, n\}$ for $n \geq 0$.
- **Morphisms:** Order-preserving maps $[m] \to [n]$.

Every morphism is a composition of **face maps** $\delta^i : [n-1] \to [n]$ (skip $i$) and **degeneracy maps** $\sigma^j : [n+1] \to [n]$ (repeat $j$). A **simplicial set** is a functor $\mathbf{\Delta}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$; a **simplicial object** in $\mathcal{C}$ is a functor $\mathbf{\Delta}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathcal{C}$.

The nerve of a category, simplicial homology, and the Dold-Kan correspondence all live here.
</Example>

<Example id="path" title="The free category on a graph (path category)">
Given a directed graph (quiver) $Q$, the **free category** $\mathbf{F}(Q)$ has:

- **Objects:** Vertices of $Q$.
- **Morphisms:** Finite directed paths in $Q$ (including empty paths as identities).
- **Composition:** Concatenation of paths.

For example, the quiver $\bullet \xrightarrow{a} \bullet \xrightarrow{b} \bullet$ generates a category with three objects and morphisms $\mathrm{id}, a, b, b \circ a$. **Representations** of quivers (functors $\mathbf{F}(Q) \to \mathbf{Vect}_k$) are a major area in representation theory — Gabriel's theorem classifies indecomposable representations for Dynkin quivers.
</Example>

<Example id="slice" title="Slice categories">
For a category $\mathcal{C}$ and an object $X$, the **slice category** (or over-category) $\mathcal{C}/X$ has:

- **Objects:** Morphisms $f: A \to X$ in $\mathcal{C}$ (think: "objects equipped with a map to $X$").
- **Morphisms:** A morphism from $(f: A \to X)$ to $(g: B \to X)$ is a map $h: A \to B$ such that $g \circ h = f$.

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
A & \xrightarrow{h} & B \\
& \searrow\scriptstyle{f} \quad \swarrow\scriptstyle{g} & \\
& X &
\end{array}
$$

Dually, the **coslice category** $X/\mathcal{C}$ has objects $X \to A$. Slice categories arise everywhere: $\mathbf{Set}/S$ is equivalent to the category of $S$-indexed families of sets; $\mathbf{Ring}/k$ for a field $k$ is the category of $k$-algebras.
</Example>

### Categories central to homological algebra

<Example id="ch-a" title="Ch(A) — the category of chain complexes">
For an abelian category $\mathcal{A}$ (e.g. $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$), the category $\mathbf{Ch}(\mathcal{A})$ has:

- **Objects:** Chain complexes $\cdots \xrightarrow{d_{n+1}} A_n \xrightarrow{d_n} A_{n-1} \xrightarrow{d_{n-1}} \cdots$ with $d_n \circ d_{n+1} = 0$.
- **Morphisms:** Chain maps $f_\bullet : A_\bullet \to B_\bullet$ (families of maps commuting with differentials).

This is again an abelian category. It is the starting point for constructing the [homotopy category](/category-theory/ch04-complexes-cohomology/concepts/c05-homotopy-category) and the [derived category](/category-theory/ch06-derived-categories/concepts/c01-derived-category).
</Example>

<Example id="k-a" title="K(A) — the homotopy category">
Same objects as $\mathbf{Ch}(\mathcal{A})$, but morphisms are chain maps modulo **chain homotopy**:

$$f \sim g \iff \exists\, s : f - g = d \circ s + s \circ d$$

$\mathbf{K}(\mathcal{A})$ is **not** abelian in general — this is a key motivation for passing to the derived category. It is, however, a **triangulated category**: the distinguished triangles come from mapping cones.
</Example>

<Example id="d-a" title="D(A) — the derived category (preview)">
Starting from $\mathbf{K}(\mathcal{A})$, we **localize** at quasi-isomorphisms (chain maps inducing isomorphisms on cohomology):

$$\mathbf{D}(\mathcal{A}) = \mathbf{K}(\mathcal{A})[\mathrm{qis}^{-1}]$$

This is the central construction in Gelfand–Manin. The derived category "remembers" only the cohomological information of complexes. Two complexes with the same cohomology become isomorphic. Derived functors ($R\Gamma$, $\mathrm{R}Hom$, $\otimes^L$) live naturally here.
</Example>

### "Exotic" categories

<Example id="discrete" title="A category with no non-identity morphisms (discrete category)">
Any set $S$ defines a **discrete category**:

- **Objects:** Elements of $S$.
- **Morphisms:** Only identities — $\mathrm{Hom}(x, y) = \varnothing$ for $x \neq y$.

A functor from a discrete category to $\mathcal{C}$ is just a family of objects of $\mathcal{C}$. The limit of such a functor is the product; the colimit is the coproduct.
</Example>

<Example id="walking-arrow" title="The walking arrow (interval category)">
The category $\mathbf{2}$ has two objects and one non-identity morphism:

$$0 \xrightarrow{!} 1$$

A functor $\mathbf{2} \to \mathcal{C}$ is just a morphism in $\mathcal{C}$. More generally, $\mathbf{n}$ (the ordinal $\{0 < 1 < \cdots < n-1\}$ as a poset category) classifies chains of $n-1$ composable morphisms. These "walking" categories are the basic shapes that index diagrams.
</Example>

<Example id="delooping" title="The delooping: from a ring to a category">
A ring $R$ can be viewed as a one-object **$\mathbf{Ab}$-enriched category** $\mathbf{B}R$:

- **Objects:** $\{*\}$.
- **Morphisms:** $\mathrm{Hom}(*,*) = R$ (an abelian group).
- **Composition:** Ring multiplication.

A left $R$-module is precisely a functor $\mathbf{B}R \to \mathbf{Ab}$. This perspective generalizes: an $\mathcal{A}$-enriched category with one object is a monoid in $\mathcal{A}$, and its "modules" are enriched functors to $\mathcal{A}$.
</Example>

## Non-examples and subtleties

<Remark title="What is NOT a category">
A common mistake is to think any collection of "things and relations" forms a category. The key requirement is **composition must be defined and associative**. For instance:

- A directed graph is not a category — there is no guarantee that a path from $A$ to $B$ and from $B$ to $C$ gives a single edge from $A$ to $C$. (But the **free category** on a graph is a category, where morphisms are paths.)
- A metric space is not obviously a category. But it can be seen as an **enriched category** over $([0,\infty], \geq, +)$: the "hom-object" $d(x,y)$ satisfies $d(x,z) \leq d(x,y) + d(y,z)$ (triangle inequality = composition) and $d(x,x) = 0$ (identity). This is Lawvere's beautiful observation.
</Remark>

<Remark title="Looking ahead">
The definition of a category is the first step. The real power comes from:

1. [Functors](/category-theory/ch01-categories-functors/concepts/c03-functor) — structure-preserving maps between categories
2. [Natural transformations](/category-theory/ch01-categories-functors/concepts/c04-natural-transformation) — maps between functors
3. [The Yoneda Lemma](/category-theory/ch01-categories-functors/theorems/t01-yoneda) — the fundamental theorem connecting objects to functors

In the Gelfand–Manin framework, these three levels organize into the **2-category** $\mathbf{Cat}$, and the entire machinery of derived categories is built by systematically manipulating these structures.
</Remark>
