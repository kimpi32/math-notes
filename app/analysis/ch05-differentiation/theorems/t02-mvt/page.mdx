# 평균값 정리 (Mean Value Theorem)

The Mean Value Theorem (MVT) is arguably the most important theorem in differential calculus. It connects the local information (derivative at a point) with the global behavior (change over an interval).

---

## Statement

<Theorem title="Mean Value Theorem" number="5.3">
Let $f : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfy:
1. $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$,
2. $f$ is differentiable on $(a, b)$.

Then there exists $c \in (a, b)$ with

$$f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}.$$
</Theorem>

<Remark>
Geometrically: there is a point where the tangent line is parallel to the secant line connecting $(a, f(a))$ and $(b, f(b))$. See the [proof](/analysis/ch05-differentiation/proofs/p01-mvt-proof).
</Remark>

---

## Immediate consequences

<Theorem title="Zero derivative implies constant">
If $f$ is differentiable on $(a, b)$ with $f'(x) = 0$ for all $x \in (a, b)$, then $f$ is constant.
</Theorem>

<Proof>
For any $x_1, x_2 \in (a, b)$ with $x_1 < x_2$: by MVT, $f(x_2) - f(x_1) = f'(c)(x_2 - x_1) = 0$. So $f(x_1) = f(x_2)$.
</Proof>

<Theorem title="Monotonicity and the derivative">
If $f$ is differentiable on $(a, b)$:
- $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x \in (a, b)$ implies $f$ is strictly increasing.
- $f'(x) < 0$ for all $x \in (a, b)$ implies $f$ is strictly decreasing.
- $f'(x) \geq 0$ for all $x$ implies $f$ is nondecreasing.
</Theorem>

## Specific computations

<Example title="Linear function">
1. $f(x) = 3x + 2$ on $[1, 4]$: $\frac{f(4) - f(1)}{3} = \frac{14 - 5}{3} = 3 = f'(x)$ for all $x$. Every point $c \in (1, 4)$ works.
</Example>

<Example title="Quadratic function">
2. $f(x) = x^2$ on $[0, 2]$: $\frac{f(2) - f(0)}{2} = \frac{4}{2} = 2$. Need $f'(c) = 2c = 2$, so $c = 1$ (the midpoint).
</Example>

<Example title="Cubic function">
3. $f(x) = x^3$ on $[0, 3]$: $\frac{27 - 0}{3} = 9$. Need $3c^2 = 9$, so $c = \sqrt{3} \approx 1.732$.
</Example>

<Example title="Exponential function">
4. $f(x) = e^x$ on $[0, 1]$: $\frac{e - 1}{1} = e - 1 \approx 1.718$. Need $e^c = e - 1$, so $c = \ln(e - 1) \approx 0.541$.
</Example>

---

## Inequality applications

<Example title="sin(x) bound">
5. $|\sin x - \sin y| \leq |x - y|$ for all $x, y$. By MVT: $\sin x - \sin y = \cos(c)(x - y)$ for some $c$. Since $|\cos c| \leq 1$: $|\sin x - \sin y| \leq |x - y|$.

This proves $\sin$ is Lipschitz with constant $1$.
</Example>

<Example title="ln(1+x) bound">
6. For $x > 0$: $\frac{x}{1+x} < \ln(1 + x) < x$.

**Upper bound**: MVT applied to $f(t) = \ln(1+t)$ on $[0, x]$: $\ln(1+x) = \frac{1}{1+c} \cdot x < x$ since $c > 0$.

**Lower bound**: $\ln(1+x) = \frac{1}{1+c} \cdot x > \frac{x}{1+x}$ since $c < x$.
</Example>

<Example title="Exponential inequality">
7. For $x > 0$: $e^x > 1 + x$. By MVT on $[0, x]$: $e^x - 1 = e^c \cdot x > 1 \cdot x = x$ since $c > 0$ gives $e^c > 1$.
</Example>

---

## Generalized MVT (Cauchy's MVT)

<Theorem title="Cauchy's Mean Value Theorem" number="5.4">
If $f, g$ are continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$ with $g'(x) \neq 0$ on $(a, b)$, then there exists $c \in (a, b)$ with

$$\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)} = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{g(b) - g(a)}.$$
</Theorem>

<Example title="Cauchy MVT for L'Hopital">
8. This is the key tool in proving L'Hopital's rule. If $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a} g(x) = 0$ and $\lim_{x \to a} f'(x)/g'(x) = L$, then by Cauchy's MVT, $\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}$ for some $c$ between $a$ and $x$, and as $x \to a$, $c \to a$, giving $\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \to L$.
</Example>

---

## L'Hopital's rule

<Theorem title="L'Hopital's Rule">
If $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a} g(x) = 0$ (or both $\to \pm\infty$), $g'(x) \neq 0$ near $a$, and $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} = L$, then $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = L$.
</Theorem>

<Example title="Standard L'Hopital applications">
9. $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x}{1} = 1$.

10. $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x - 1}{x} = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x}{1} = 1$.

11. $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1/x}{1} = 0$.
</Example>

---

## MVT and antiderivatives

<Example title="Two antiderivatives differ by a constant">
12. If $F'(x) = G'(x)$ for all $x \in (a, b)$, then $(F - G)'(x) = 0$, so $F - G$ is constant (by the MVT consequence). This fundamental fact underlies the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: all antiderivatives of $f$ differ by a constant.
</Example>
