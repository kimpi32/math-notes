# 평균값 정리의 증명 (Proof of the Mean Value Theorem)

The proof reduces the MVT to Rolle's theorem via an auxiliary function that "tilts" the graph to make the endpoints equal.

---

## Theorem restated

<Theorem title="Mean Value Theorem">
If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$, then there exists $c \in (a, b)$ with

$$f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}.$$
</Theorem>

---

## Full proof

<Proof>
**Define the auxiliary function.** Let $L(x)$ be the linear function (secant line) connecting $(a, f(a))$ to $(b, f(b))$:

$$L(x) = f(a) + \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}(x - a).$$

Define $g(x) = f(x) - L(x)$.

**Verify Rolle's hypotheses.**

1. *Continuity*: $g$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ (difference of continuous functions).

2. *Differentiability*: $g$ is differentiable on $(a, b)$ with

$$g'(x) = f'(x) - \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}.$$

3. *Equal endpoints*:

$$g(a) = f(a) - L(a) = f(a) - f(a) = 0,$$

$$g(b) = f(b) - L(b) = f(b) - \left[f(a) + \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}(b - a)\right] = f(b) - f(b) = 0.$$

**Apply Rolle's theorem.** By Rolle's theorem, there exists $c \in (a, b)$ with $g'(c) = 0$:

$$0 = f'(c) - \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}.$$

Therefore $f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}$.
</Proof>

---

## Geometric intuition

<Remark>
The function $g(x) = f(x) - L(x)$ measures the **vertical distance** from the graph of $f$ to the secant line. Since $g(a) = g(b) = 0$, Rolle's theorem guarantees that $g$ has a horizontal tangent at some interior point $c$. A horizontal tangent of $g$ means $f'(c) = L'(c) = $ slope of secant, which is exactly the MVT conclusion.
</Remark>

---

## Why this auxiliary function?

<Remark>
The key idea is: subtracting the secant line "straightens" the problem. After subtracting, the endpoints are equal, and Rolle's theorem applies directly. This technique of reducing a general problem to a special case (here, MVT to Rolle's) is ubiquitous in analysis.
</Remark>

## Verifying the proof with examples

<Example title="f(x) = x^2 on [1, 3]">
1. $L(x) = 1 + \frac{9 - 1}{2}(x - 1) = 4x - 3$. $g(x) = x^2 - 4x + 3 = (x-1)(x-3)$. $g'(x) = 2x - 4 = 0$ at $c = 2$. Check: $f'(2) = 4 = \frac{9-1}{2}$.
</Example>

<Example title="f(x) = sin(x) on [0, pi]">
2. $L(x) = 0 + \frac{0 - 0}{\pi}(x - 0) = 0$. $g(x) = \sin x$. $g'(x) = \cos x = 0$ at $c = \pi/2$. Check: $f'(\pi/2) = 0 = \frac{0-0}{\pi}$.

Here the MVT reduces to Rolle's theorem since $f(0) = f(\pi) = 0$.
</Example>

<Example title="f(x) = e^x on [0, 1]">
3. $L(x) = 1 + (e - 1)x$. $g(x) = e^x - 1 - (e-1)x$. $g'(x) = e^x - (e-1) = 0$ at $x = \ln(e - 1) \approx 0.541$.

Check: $f'(c) = e^c = e - 1 \approx 1.718 = \frac{e - 1}{1}$.
</Example>

## Proof of consequences

<Example title="Proving f' = 0 implies f constant">
4. Let $f'(x) = 0$ for all $x \in (a, b)$. Take any $x_1 < x_2$ in $(a, b)$. By MVT:

$$f(x_2) - f(x_1) = f'(c)(x_2 - x_1) = 0.$$

So $f(x_1) = f(x_2)$.
</Example>

<Example title="Proving Lipschitz bound">
5. If $|f'(x)| \leq M$ for all $x \in (a, b)$, then for $x_1, x_2 \in (a, b)$:

$$|f(x_2) - f(x_1)| = |f'(c)| \cdot |x_2 - x_1| \leq M|x_2 - x_1|.$$

So $f$ is Lipschitz with constant $M$.
</Example>

<Example title="Proving monotonicity">
6. If $f'(x) > 0$ on $(a, b)$: for $x_1 < x_2$, $f(x_2) - f(x_1) = f'(c)(x_2 - x_1) > 0$. So $f$ is strictly increasing.
</Example>

---

## Proof of Cauchy's MVT

<Example title="Cauchy MVT proof">
7. To prove Cauchy's MVT ($\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)} = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{g(b)-g(a)}$), define:

$$h(x) = f(x)[g(b) - g(a)] - g(x)[f(b) - f(a)].$$

Then $h(a) = f(a)g(b) - g(a)f(b) = h(b)$, so Rolle's theorem gives $h'(c) = 0$:

$$f'(c)[g(b) - g(a)] = g'(c)[f(b) - f(a)].$$
</Example>

---

## Applications of the proof technique

<Example title="Generalized MVT for integrals">
8. The Mean Value Theorem for integrals states: if $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, there exists $c \in (a,b)$ with $\int_a^b f = f(c)(b-a)$. This follows from the MVT applied to the antiderivative $F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$: $F(b) - F(a) = F'(c)(b - a) = f(c)(b - a)$.
</Example>

<Example title="Newton's method convergence">
9. The MVT is used to prove quadratic convergence of Newton's method. If $f(r) = 0$ and $x_n$ is near $r$:

$$x_{n+1} - r = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)} - r = \frac{f'(x_n)(x_n - r) - f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}.$$

Using Taylor: $f(x_n) = f'(c)(x_n - r)$, so the error involves $|f'(x_n) - f'(c)|$, which by MVT on $f'$ is $\leq |f''(\xi)||x_n - r|$.
</Example>

<Example title="Convexity">
10. If $f''(x) > 0$ on $(a,b)$, then $f$ is convex. Proof: by MVT, $\frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} = f'(c_1)$ and $\frac{f(b) - f(x)}{b - x} = f'(c_2)$ with $c_1 < x < c_2$. Since $f'' > 0$ implies $f'$ is increasing: $f'(c_1) < f'(c_2)$, which gives the convexity inequality.
</Example>

<Example title="Comparing functions via derivatives">
11. To prove $e^x > 1 + x + x^2/2$ for $x > 0$: let $g(x) = e^x - 1 - x - x^2/2$. Then $g(0) = 0$ and $g'(x) = e^x - 1 - x$. Now $g'(0) = 0$ and $g''(x) = e^x - 1 > 0$ for $x > 0$. By MVT, $g'(x) > 0$ for $x > 0$, then $g(x) > 0$ for $x > 0$.
</Example>

<Example title="Bernoulli inequality">
12. For $x > -1$ and $n \geq 1$: $(1 + x)^n \geq 1 + nx$. Let $f(x) = (1+x)^n - 1 - nx$. Then $f(0) = 0$ and $f'(x) = n(1+x)^{n-1} - n = n((1+x)^{n-1} - 1)$. For $x > 0$: $(1+x)^{n-1} > 1$, so $f' > 0$ and $f(x) > f(0) = 0$.
</Example>
