# 조임 정리 (Squeeze Theorem)

The Squeeze Theorem (also called the Sandwich Theorem or Pinching Theorem) is a fundamental tool for evaluating limits when direct computation is difficult but good bounds are available.

---

## Statement

<Theorem title="Squeeze Theorem for Functions" number="3.3">
Let $f, g, h$ be functions defined on a set $A$ and let $c$ be a limit point of $A$. Suppose:

1. $g(x) \leq f(x) \leq h(x)$ for all $x \in A$ with $x \neq c$ (or at least for $x$ in some punctured neighborhood of $c$).
2. $\lim_{x \to c} g(x) = \lim_{x \to c} h(x) = L$.

Then $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L$.
</Theorem>

<Remark>
The theorem also holds for one-sided limits ($x \to c^+$, $x \to c^-$) and limits at infinity ($x \to \infty$, $x \to -\infty$) with the obvious modifications. See the [proof](/analysis/ch03-limits/proofs/p01-squeeze-proof) for details.
</Remark>

---

## Classic applications

<Example title="The fundamental trigonometric limit">
1. $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$.

For $0 < |x| < \pi/2$, geometric arguments give $\cos x \leq \frac{\sin x}{x} \leq 1$. Since $\lim_{x \to 0} \cos x = 1$ and the upper bound is $1$, the Squeeze Theorem gives $\frac{\sin x}{x} \to 1$.
</Example>

<Example title="Cosine limit from sine">
2. $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{x^2} = \frac{1}{2}$.

Using $1 - \cos x = 2\sin^2(x/2)$:

$$\frac{1 - \cos x}{x^2} = \frac{2\sin^2(x/2)}{x^2} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \left(\frac{\sin(x/2)}{x/2}\right)^2 \to \frac{1}{2} \cdot 1^2 = \frac{1}{2}.$$
</Example>

<Example title="Squeeze with oscillation">
3. $\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 \sin(1/x) = 0$.

Since $-1 \leq \sin(1/x) \leq 1$, we have $-x^2 \leq x^2 \sin(1/x) \leq x^2$. Both bounds tend to $0$, so $x^2\sin(1/x) \to 0$.
</Example>

<Example title="x cos(1/x) at zero">
4. $\lim_{x \to 0} x\cos(1/x) = 0$.

$|x\cos(1/x)| \leq |x| \to 0$. Equivalently: $-|x| \leq x\cos(1/x) \leq |x|$, and both bounds tend to $0$.
</Example>

---

## Squeeze with exponential and logarithmic bounds

<Example title="Exponential dominates polynomial">
5. $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^n}{e^x} = 0$ for any $n \in \mathbb{N}$.

For $x > 2n$: $e^x > \frac{x^{2n}}{(2n)!}$ (from the Taylor series), so $0 < \frac{x^n}{e^x} < \frac{(2n)!}{x^n} \to 0$.
</Example>

<Example title="Logarithm grows slower than any power">
6. $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x^\alpha} = 0$ for any $\alpha > 0$.

Set $x = e^t$: $\frac{\ln x}{x^\alpha} = \frac{t}{e^{\alpha t}} \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$ by Example 5 (with $n = 1$).
</Example>

---

## Squeeze at infinity

<Example title="Bounded oscillation at infinity">
7. $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 0$.

$-\frac{1}{x} \leq \frac{\sin x}{x} \leq \frac{1}{x}$, and $\frac{1}{x} \to 0$ as $x \to \infty$.
</Example>

<Example title="Cesaro-type limit">
8. $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sin(x) + \cos(x)}{x^2 + 1} = 0$.

$|\sin x + \cos x| \leq 2$, so $\left|\frac{\sin x + \cos x}{x^2 + 1}\right| \leq \frac{2}{x^2 + 1} \to 0$.
</Example>

---

## Squeeze for one-sided limits

<Example title="Right-hand squeeze">
9. $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \sqrt{x} \sin(1/x) = 0$.

$|\sqrt{x}\sin(1/x)| \leq \sqrt{x} \to 0$ as $x \to 0^+$.
</Example>

<Example title="Left-hand squeeze">
10. $\lim_{x \to 0^-} x \cdot e^{1/x} = 0$.

For $x < 0$: $e^{1/x} = e^{-|1/x|} < 1$, so $|x \cdot e^{1/x}| < |x| \to 0$.
</Example>

---

## When squeeze does not apply directly

<Example title="Need matching bounds">
11. For $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x}$, the naive bound $-1 \leq \frac{\sin x}{x} \leq 1$ does not help (it does not squeeze to a single value). We need the sharper bound $\cos x \leq \frac{\sin x}{x} \leq 1$, which requires geometric reasoning with the unit circle.
</Example>

<Example title="Squeeze combined with algebraic manipulation">
12. $\lim_{x \to \infty} \left(\sqrt{x^2 + x} - x\right) = \frac{1}{2}$.

Multiply by the conjugate: $\sqrt{x^2 + x} - x = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 + x} + x}$. For $x > 0$:

$$\frac{x}{x\sqrt{1 + 1/x} + x} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + 1/x} + 1}.$$

Since $\frac{1}{x} \to 0$, the denominator $\to \sqrt{1} + 1 = 2$, so the limit is $\frac{1}{2}$. The squeeze provides: for $x > 1$, $\frac{1}{2 + 1/\sqrt{x}} < \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+1/x}+1} < \frac{1}{2}$, and the lower bound $\to \frac{1}{2}$.
</Example>
