# 함수의 극한 (Limit of a Function)

The limit of a function extends the idea of sequential convergence to the setting of real-valued functions. It is the gateway to continuity, differentiation, and integration.

---

## Limit points

<Definition title="Limit point of a set" number="3.1">
A point $c \in \mathbb{R}$ is a **limit point** (or **accumulation point**) of a set $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ if for every $\delta > 0$, the punctured neighborhood $(c - \delta, c + \delta) \setminus \{c\}$ contains at least one point of $A$.

Equivalently, $c$ is a limit point of $A$ if there exists a sequence $(x_n)$ in $A \setminus \{c\}$ with $x_n \to c$.
</Definition>

<Example title="Limit points of sets">
1. The limit points of $(0, 1)$ are the closed interval $[0, 1]$. Every point in $[0,1]$ can be approached by a sequence in $(0,1) \setminus \{c\}$.

2. The limit points of $\mathbb{N} = \{1, 2, 3, \ldots\}$ form the empty set. No real number can be approached by a sequence of distinct natural numbers in a punctured neighborhood.

3. The limit points of $\{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ are $\{0\}$. The point $0$ is approached by $1/n \to 0$.
</Example>

---

## Definition of limit

<Definition title="Limit of a function (epsilon-delta)" number="3.2">
Let $f : A \to \mathbb{R}$ and let $c$ be a limit point of $A$. We say $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L$ if:

$$\forall \varepsilon > 0, \quad \exists \delta > 0, \quad \forall x \in A : \quad 0 < |x - c| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon.$$

Note: the value $f(c)$ is irrelevant (and $f$ need not even be defined at $c$). Only the behavior of $f$ near $c$ matters.
</Definition>

<Example title="Basic limit computations">
4. $\lim_{x \to 2} (3x + 1) = 7$.

**Proof**: Let $\varepsilon > 0$. We need $|3x + 1 - 7| = 3|x - 2| < \varepsilon$. Choose $\delta = \varepsilon / 3$. Then $0 < |x - 2| < \delta$ implies $|3x + 1 - 7| = 3|x-2| < 3\delta = \varepsilon$.

5. $\lim_{x \to 3} x^2 = 9$.

**Proof**: $|x^2 - 9| = |x-3| \cdot |x+3|$. If $|x - 3| < 1$, then $|x+3| < 7$. Choose $\delta = \min(1, \varepsilon/7)$. Then $|x^2 - 9| < 7 \cdot \varepsilon/7 = \varepsilon$.
</Example>

## Sequential characterization

<Theorem title="Sequential characterization of limits" number="3.1">
Let $c$ be a limit point of $A$ and $f : A \to \mathbb{R}$. Then

$$\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L$$

if and only if for every sequence $(x_n)$ in $A \setminus \{c\}$ with $x_n \to c$, we have $f(x_n) \to L$.
</Theorem>

<Example title="Using sequences to find limits">
6. $\lim_{x \to 0} x \sin(1/x) = 0$.

For any $x_n \to 0$ with $x_n \neq 0$: $|x_n \sin(1/x_n)| \leq |x_n| \to 0$.
</Example>

<Example title="Using sequences to prove nonexistence">
7. $\lim_{x \to 0} \sin(1/x)$ does not exist.

Take $x_n = \frac{1}{2\pi n} \to 0$: $f(x_n) = \sin(2\pi n) = 0$.
Take $y_n = \frac{1}{2\pi n + \pi/2} \to 0$: $f(y_n) = \sin(2\pi n + \pi/2) = 1$.
Since $0 \neq 1$, the limit does not exist.
</Example>

## One-sided limits

<Definition title="One-sided limits" number="3.3">
The **right-hand limit** $\lim_{x \to c^+} f(x) = L$ means:

$$\forall \varepsilon > 0, \quad \exists \delta > 0, \quad \forall x \in A: \quad 0 < x - c < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon.$$

The **left-hand limit** $\lim_{x \to c^-} f(x) = L$ is defined analogously with $0 < c - x < \delta$.

The two-sided limit exists if and only if both one-sided limits exist and are equal.
</Definition>

<Example title="One-sided limits">
8. $f(x) = \frac{|x|}{x}$ (the sign function for $x \neq 0$):

$\lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x) = 1$ and $\lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x) = -1$.

The two-sided limit $\lim_{x \to 0} f(x)$ does not exist.
</Example>

<Example title="Floor function limits">
9. $f(x) = \lfloor x \rfloor$ (floor function):

$\lim_{x \to 2^+} \lfloor x \rfloor = 2$ and $\lim_{x \to 2^-} \lfloor x \rfloor = 1$.

At non-integers, both one-sided limits equal the floor value.
</Example>

---

## Limits at infinity

<Definition title="Limit at infinity" number="3.4">
$\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L$ means:

$$\forall \varepsilon > 0, \quad \exists M > 0, \quad \forall x > M : \quad |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon.$$

Similarly, $\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L$ uses $x < -M$.
</Definition>

<Example title="Limits at infinity">
10. $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0$: given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $M = 1/\varepsilon$.

11. $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 + 1}{2x^2 - 3} = \frac{1}{2}$: divide numerator and denominator by $x^2$.
</Example>

---

## Infinite limits

<Definition title="Infinite limits" number="3.5">
$\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = +\infty$ means:

$$\forall M > 0, \quad \exists \delta > 0, \quad \forall x : \quad 0 < |x - c| < \delta \implies f(x) > M.$$
</Definition>

<Example title="Infinite limit example">
12. $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x^2} = +\infty$: given $M > 0$, choose $\delta = 1/\sqrt{M}$. Then $0 < |x| < \delta$ implies $\frac{1}{x^2} > \frac{1}{\delta^2} = M$.
</Example>

---

## Summary of limit types

<Remark>
The different types of limits ($x \to c$, $x \to c^\pm$, $x \to \pm\infty$, $f(x) \to L$, $f(x) \to \pm\infty$) all follow the same logical pattern: a quantifier alternation $\forall \varepsilon\; \exists \delta$ (or $\forall M\; \exists \delta$, etc.). Mastering this pattern for one case makes all others straightforward.
</Remark>
