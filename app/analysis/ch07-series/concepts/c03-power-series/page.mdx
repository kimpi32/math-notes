# Power Series

Power series are infinite polynomials. They provide the bridge between analysis and algebra, representing functions as infinite sums and enabling term-by-term differentiation and integration.

---

## Definition and radius of convergence

<Definition title="Power series" number="7.8">
A **power series** centered at $c$ is a series of the form

$$\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n (x - c)^n = a_0 + a_1(x-c) + a_2(x-c)^2 + \cdots$$

where $(a_n)$ is a sequence of real coefficients and $x$ is a variable.
</Definition>

<Definition title="Radius of convergence" number="7.9">
The **radius of convergence** of a power series $\sum a_n (x - c)^n$ is the number $R \in [0, \infty]$ defined by

$$\frac{1}{R} = \limsup_{n \to \infty} |a_n|^{1/n}$$

(with conventions $1/0 = \infty$ and $1/\infty = 0$). The series converges absolutely for $|x - c| < R$ and diverges for $|x - c| > R$.
</Definition>

<Theorem title="Hadamard's formula" number="7.10">
The power series $\sum a_n(x - c)^n$ has radius of convergence $R$ given by $1/R = \limsup |a_n|^{1/n}$. Moreover:

- For $|x - c| < R$: the series converges absolutely.
- For $|x - c| > R$: the series diverges.
- For $|x - c| = R$: no general conclusion (must be checked case by case).
</Theorem>

<Example title="Geometric series as a power series">
$\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n$: here $a_n = 1$, so $|a_n|^{1/n} = 1$ and $R = 1$. Converges for $|x| < 1$ to $\frac{1}{1-x}$. At $x = 1$: $\sum 1$ diverges. At $x = -1$: $\sum (-1)^n$ diverges.
</Example>

<Example title="Exponential series">
$\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!}$: $a_n = \frac{1}{n!}$, so $|a_n|^{1/n} = \frac{1}{(n!)^{1/n}} \to 0$ (by Stirling). Thus $R = \infty$. The series converges for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$ and equals $e^x$.
</Example>

<Example title="Radius of convergence via ratio test">
For $\sum \frac{x^n}{n!}$: $\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{n!}{(n+1)!} = \frac{1}{n+1} \to 0$. So $R = 1/0 = \infty$.

For $\sum n! \, x^n$: $\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = n+1 \to \infty$. So $R = 0$ (converges only at $x = 0$).

For $\sum \frac{x^n}{n}$: $\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{n}{n+1} \to 1$. So $R = 1$.
</Example>

## Behavior at the boundary

<Example title="Different behavior at endpoints">
Consider $\sum \frac{x^n}{n^p}$ centered at $0$ with $R = 1$:

- $p = 0$: $\sum x^n$. At $x = 1$: $\sum 1$ diverges. At $x = -1$: $\sum (-1)^n$ diverges.
- $p = 1$: $\sum \frac{x^n}{n}$. At $x = 1$: $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ diverges. At $x = -1$: $\sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n}$ converges (conditionally).
- $p = 2$: $\sum \frac{x^n}{n^2}$. At $x = 1$: $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges. At $x = -1$: $\sum \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$ converges (absolutely).

The interval of convergence can be open, half-open, or closed at each endpoint.
</Example>

## Standard power series

<Example title="Common Taylor series (centered at 0)">
$$e^x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!}, \qquad R = \infty$$

$$\sin x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}, \qquad R = \infty$$

$$\cos x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}, \qquad R = \infty$$

$$\ln(1+x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1} x^n}{n}, \qquad R = 1$$

$$\frac{1}{1-x} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n, \qquad R = 1$$

$$\arctan x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{2n+1}, \qquad R = 1$$
</Example>

## Operations on power series

<Theorem title="Term-by-term differentiation" number="7.11">
If $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-c)^n$ has radius of convergence $R > 0$, then $f$ is differentiable on $(c-R, c+R)$ and

$$f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n \, a_n (x-c)^{n-1},$$

with the same radius of convergence $R$.
</Theorem>

<Example title="Differentiating the geometric series">
$\frac{1}{1-x} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n$. Differentiating:

$$\frac{1}{(1-x)^2} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n x^{n-1} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (n+1)x^n.$$

At $x = 1/2$: $\frac{1}{(1/2)^2} = 4 = \sum (n+1)/2^n$.
</Example>

<Theorem title="Term-by-term integration" number="7.12">
If $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-c)^n$ has radius of convergence $R > 0$, then

$$\int_c^x f(t)\,dt = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{a_n}{n+1}(x-c)^{n+1},$$

with the same radius of convergence.
</Theorem>

<Example title="Deriving ln(1+x) from the geometric series">
$\frac{1}{1+t} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-t)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-1)^n t^n$ for $|t| < 1$.

Integrating from $0$ to $x$:

$$\ln(1+x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{n+1}}{n+1} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1} x^n}{n}$$

for $|x| < 1$. (Also valid at $x = 1$ by Abel's theorem.)
</Example>

<Example title="Deriving arctan x from the geometric series">
$\frac{1}{1+t^2} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-1)^n t^{2n}$ for $|t| < 1$.

Integrating from $0$ to $x$:

$$\arctan x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{2n+1}$$

for $|x| < 1$. Setting $x = 1$ (valid by Abel's theorem): $\frac{\pi}{4} = 1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{7} + \cdots$ (Leibniz formula).
</Example>

## Abel's theorem

<Theorem title="Abel's theorem" number="7.13">
If $\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n$ converges to $S$, then $\lim_{x \to 1^-} \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n = S$.

In other words, if the power series converges at the boundary point $x = R$, then the function defined by the power series is continuous from the left at $x = R$.
</Theorem>

<Example title="Abel's theorem applied to ln 2">
$\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n} = \ln 2$ (this converges at $x = 1$). Abel's theorem confirms:

$$\lim_{x \to 1^-} \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1} x^n}{n} = \lim_{x \to 1^-} \ln(1 + x) = \ln 2.$$
</Example>

## Uniqueness of power series

<Theorem title="Identity theorem for power series" number="7.14">
If $\sum a_n x^n = \sum b_n x^n$ for all $x$ in some interval $(-\delta, \delta)$ with $\delta > 0$, then $a_n = b_n$ for all $n$.
</Theorem>

<Example title="Determining coefficients">
If $e^x = \sum a_n x^n$, then by the identity theorem, $a_n = \frac{1}{n!}$ is the only possibility. Differentiating $n$ times and evaluating at $0$: $a_n = \frac{f^{(n)}(0)}{n!}$, which gives the Taylor coefficient formula.
</Example>

<Example title="Power series for (1+x)^alpha (binomial series)">
For any $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$(1+x)^\alpha = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \binom{\alpha}{n} x^n, \qquad |x| < 1$$

where $\binom{\alpha}{n} = \frac{\alpha(\alpha-1)\cdots(\alpha-n+1)}{n!}$. For $\alpha = -1$: $\frac{1}{1+x} = \sum (-x)^n$. For $\alpha = 1/2$: $\sqrt{1+x} = 1 + \frac{x}{2} - \frac{x^2}{8} + \cdots$
</Example>

<Example title="Finding a power series by long division">
$\frac{1}{1-x-x^2}$ (generating function for Fibonacci numbers): write $\frac{1}{1-x-x^2} = \sum F_n x^n$ where $F_0 = F_1 = 1$ and $F_{n+2} = F_{n+1} + F_n$. The radius of convergence is $R = \frac{2}{1+\sqrt{5}} = \frac{1}{\varphi}$ where $\varphi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$ is the golden ratio.
</Example>

<Remark>
Power series are the most well-behaved infinite series: they converge absolutely in the interior of their interval, can be differentiated and integrated term by term, and represent analytic (infinitely differentiable) functions. The study of uniform convergence in [Chapter 8](/analysis/ch08-sequences-of-functions/concepts/c02-uniform-convergence) explains why these operations are justified.
</Remark>
